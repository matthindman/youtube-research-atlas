# YouTube Research Questions and Unknowns Report

## Scope and framing

This report reviews the YouTube census project memo and the archive's 2022-and-later scholarly work where YouTube is a substantive object of analysis. For each article, the summary focuses on how the paper frames the most important research questions, what it identifies as unknown, and where it sees the literature as incomplete. Pure methods papers, broad social-media surveys, and descriptive industry reports are not summarized article-by-article unless they make a direct argument about YouTube research gaps.

## Memo framing: `youtube_census_project_memo_claude.md`

The memo argues that the central unresolved task in YouTube scholarship is to move from fragmented case studies toward platform-wide measurement of YouTube as an attention system. It treats the highest-value questions as descriptive and structural before they are causal: how concentrated attention is, what kinds of institutions capture it, how much of the platform is visible from a 10k+ or 50k+ channel panel, how contestable the hierarchy is, how precarious creator attention really is, and how much the platform differs across languages, formats, and event moments.

The memo also makes a methodological claim: a forward-looking near-census channel panel would not solve user-level questions about persuasion or recommendation pathways, but it would solve a more foundational problem by enabling independent measurement of concentration, mobility, taxonomy, event response, and creator-market structure. In that sense, the memo positions the biggest unknowns as platform-wide descriptive unknowns, not only recommender-system unknowns.

## Overall summary, with direct textual support

The six claims below synthesize the archive's main arguments. Supporting quotations are ordered from the most direct and central support to the more indirect but still relevant support. Page references are PDF page numbers.

### 1. Platform-wide description still lags YouTube's importance

Across the archive, the most consistent diagnosis is that YouTube matters enormously but remains weakly described at the platform level. The field still lacks a stable macro-description of channel structure, attention concentration, and platform-wide composition.

Supporting quotes:
1. "Academic research has not, however, caught up to this fact." (Munger, Bisbee, Yalcin, Phillips, & Hindman, 2025, PDF p. 2)
2. "many of its fundamental characteristics remain unknown." (McGrady, Zheng, Curran, Baumgartner, & Zuckerman, 2023, PDF p. 1)
3. "scant scholarly attention has been directed toward YouTube as a news source." (Reveilhac, 2024, PDF p. 1)
4. "it has not yet captured much interest of Political Scientists." (Munger & Phillips, 2022, PDF p. 6)

### 2. Sampling, discovery, taxonomy, and measurement remain foundational bottlenecks

A second recurring claim is that researchers still struggle to observe YouTube in representative and scalable ways. Discovery methods skew toward large channels, hand-coded samples are small, and channel or video classification remains an active methodological problem rather than settled infrastructure.

Supporting quotes:
1. "Most existing research focuses only on the most popular channels." (Zaitsev & Clark, 2025, PDF p. 1)
2. "Existing research on YouTube has tended to rely on bespoke data sets" (Lai, Brown, Bisbee, Tucker, Nagler, & Bonneau, 2024, PDF p. 2)
3. "Part of the challenge in reconciling this debate stems from data limitations." (Brown, Bisbee, Lai, Bonneau, Nagler, & Tucker, 2022, PDF p. 2)
4. "laborious manual annotation" (Boesinger, Ribeiro, Veselovsky, & West, 2024, PDF p. 1)
5. "low-recall methods such as keyword search." (Boesinger et al., 2024, PDF p. 1)
6. YouTube is "almost inscrutable" to researchers. (McGrady et al., 2023, PDF p. 3)

### 3. Recommendation effects remain difficult to identify cleanly and increasingly look conditional rather than universal

The post-2022 literature does not support one simple rabbit-hole story. Instead, it repeatedly emphasizes identification problems, incomplete observability, and the need to separate algorithmic supply from user intention, subscriptions, referrals, and downstream attitudinal effects.

Supporting quotes:
1. "lack of appropriate counterfactuals" (Hosseinmardi, Ghasemian, Rivera, Lanas, Ribeiro, West, & Watts, 2024, PDF p. 1)
2. "cannot disentangle the effects of the algorithm from a user's intentions." (Hosseinmardi et al., 2024, PDF p. 1)
3. "evidence on these issues is inconclusive." (Haroon, Wojcieszak, Chhabra, Liu, Mohapatra, & Shafiq, 2023, PDF p. 2)
4. "limited access and an incomplete understanding of the YouTube recommender system" (Yesilada & Lewandowsky, 2022, PDF p. 1)
5. "few existing research designs attempt to connect" (Liu, Hu, Savas, Baum, Berinsky, Chaney, Lucas, Mariman, de Benedictis-Kessner, Guess, Knox, & Stewart, 2025, PDF p. 2)

### 4. Creator labor, monetization, and governance are central research agendas, not secondary ones

Recent scholarship treats YouTube not only as an information intermediary but also as a labor market and governance system. The unresolved questions concern unequal earnings, monetization thresholds, public accountability, copyright enforcement, and the limited formal role creators have in governance.

Supporting quotes:
1. "lack of comprehensive quantitative studies exploring earnings on YouTube" (Verwiebe, Osorio-Krauter, Fritsch, Weissmann, Philipp, & Buder, 2025, PDF p. 2)
2. "non-trivial governance challenge" (Abou El-Komboz, Kerkhof, & Loh, 2023, PDF p. 5)
3. "approaches are difficult to scale" (Hallinan, Reynolds, Kuperberg, & Rothenstein, 2025, PDF p. 3)
4. "formalised process of stakeholder participation" (Hallinan et al., 2025, PDF p. 7)
5. "make accountability difficult." (Reynolds & Hallinan, 2024, PDF p. 3)
6. "one of the primary ways creators challenge the power imbalance" (Hallinan, Reynolds, & Rothenstein, 2024, PDF p. 2)
7. "highly unequal platformed media system" (Rieder, Borra, Coromina, & Matamoros-Fernandez, 2023, PDF p. 2)
8. "widen their income streams." (Rieder et al., 2023, PDF p. 2)

### 5. News and public-affairs YouTube is a blurred hybrid space spanning mainstream, alternative, and entertainment logics

The archive's newer work on news and politics rejects simple binaries. It emphasizes blurred institutional boundaries, multiple channel types, overlap between news and entertainment, and the possibility that some YouTube spaces are more cross-cutting than strong echo-chamber accounts assume.

Supporting quotes:
1. "line between traditional news channels and alternative news channels is blurring." (Reveilhac, 2024, PDF p. 1)
2. "Five clusters of alternative news channels are identified" (Reveilhac, 2024, PDF p. 1)
3. "most users go to platforms for entertainment, not for verified news" (Yu, Haroon, Menchen-Trevino, & Wojcieszak, 2024, PDF p. 2)
4. "algorithms actively direct users to entertainment and away from news" (Yu et al., 2024, PDF p. 2)
5. "be a solution to those echo chambers" (Chae & Lee, 2024, PDF p. 4)

### 6. Rapid changes in format and platform governance make YouTube a moving target for scholarship

Several papers stress that the object of study itself keeps shifting. Shorts, changing monetization rules, and policy adjustments mean that older conclusions may not travel cleanly to the current platform.

Supporting quotes:
1. "Are short videos replacing longer videos on YouTube" (Violot, Elmas, Bilogrevic, & Humbert, 2024, PDF p. 2)
2. "first comprehensive study of YouTube Shorts" (Violot et al., 2024, PDF p. 2)
3. "Short-form videos are currently understudied." (Violot et al., 2024, PDF p. 10)
4. "as a platform evolves over time, the criteria for access may have to be adjusted as well" (Abou El-Komboz et al., 2023, PDF p. 5)
5. "This, however, can be a disruptive event" (Abou El-Komboz et al., 2023, PDF p. 5)
6. "negative coverage significantly predicts changes to platforms' user policies." (Marchal, Hoes, Kluser, Hamborg, Alizadeh, Kubli, & Katzenbach, 2025, PDF p. 2)

## Article summaries

### 2025

#### Zaitsev & Clark (2025), *Classifying YouTube channels: Algorithmic approach to channel mapping*
This paper treats scale and discovery as the basic bottlenecks in YouTube scholarship. Its point of departure is that most studies concentrate on the most visible channels and therefore miss smaller but still politically consequential creators. The key research question is how to classify channels at scale without reproducing the selection bias that comes from focusing only on the head of the distribution. The paper therefore frames the major unknown as the structure of political and cultural YouTube beyond the most popular channels: who is there, how they should be categorized, and how conclusions change once channel discovery and classification improve.

#### Verwiebe et al. (2025), *Working on an algorithm-controlled platform as a content creator: What explains unequal earnings on YouTube?*
Verwiebe et al. position earnings inequality as a major but weakly measured dimension of the creator economy. They argue that most work on YouTube labor emphasizes platform dependence and precarity but lacks comprehensive quantitative evidence on how earnings are distributed and what explains that distribution. Their main unknown is therefore not merely whether inequality exists, but how much of it is explained by platform variables versus conventional stratification mechanisms such as gender, education, age, and migration background. The paper pushes the literature toward a broader question about whether YouTube reproduces or transforms familiar social inequalities inside an algorithmically governed labor market.

#### Munger, Bisbee, Yalcin, Phillips, & Hindman (2025), *Pressing play on politics: Quantitative description of YouTube*
This article explicitly frames YouTube as critically important but descriptively understudied. It argues that the field still lacks basic quantitative description of political YouTube at scale, including who produces content, who captures attention, and how comments and viewers are distributed. The major unknowns it foregrounds are platform structure questions: concentration, community segmentation, the role of prolific commenters, and the mismatch between comment publics and viewership publics. The paper implies that one of the literature's biggest weaknesses is its tendency to jump directly to recommender effects without first establishing a reliable descriptive baseline for the political system sitting on top of YouTube.

#### McGrady, Zheng, & Zuckerman (2025), *One Platform, Four Languages: Comparing English, Spanish, Hindi, and Russian YouTube*
McGrady and coauthors frame Anglocentrism as one of the field's most important blind spots. Their starting question is whether YouTube can be treated as a single global system when linguistic communities may differ sharply in scale, institutional composition, and content organization. The paper identifies several unknowns: where language boundaries lie, how language should proxy geography, and how much existing English-language theory generalizes to non-English YouTube. Its broader contribution is to recast YouTube scholarship as comparative platform research, where one of the major unanswered questions is how many distinct "YouTubes" coexist within the same infrastructure.

#### Liu et al. (2025), *Short-term exposure to filter-bubble recommendation systems has limited polarization effects*
Liu et al. frame the main gap as a disconnect between studies of algorithmic supply and studies of downstream political effects. They argue that even when researchers can show that recommendation systems alter exposure, few designs trace that exposure through to actual watching behavior, attitudes, or polarization outcomes. The key unknown is therefore not only whether recommendation systems can produce polarized media diets, but whether those diets cause meaningful short-run changes in beliefs, affective polarization, or democratic attitudes. The article places YouTube scholarship within a broader unresolved debate about the size of algorithmic effects relative to user predispositions.

#### Hallinan, Reynolds, Kuperberg, & Rothenstein (2025), *Aspirational platform governance: How creators legitimise content moderation through accusations of bias*
This paper argues that platform governance research has paid too little attention to public, creator-led contestation happening on platforms themselves. Its main question is how creators articulate claims of bias and legitimacy when formal participation in governance is weak or absent. The central unknowns concern the social life of governance: how moderation rules are interpreted by creators, how accusations of bias become collective rather than isolated grievances, and how legitimacy is constructed from below. The paper broadens the field beyond takedowns and recommender audits toward a still underdeveloped research agenda on governance as a lived and publicly negotiated process.

### 2024

#### Munger (2024), *The YouTube Apparatus*
Munger's book is a meta-argument about the shape of the field. It claims YouTube is understudied relative to its scale because data access, disciplinary preferences, and media panics have steered scholarship toward narrower and more tractable questions. The core unknowns, as Munger frames them, are the platform's ordinary political economy and social structure: how attention is allocated, how creators and audiences are organized, and how the platform fits into a wider media system. The book therefore treats the biggest research gap as agenda-setting failure: scholarship has overinvested in the rabbit-hole narrative and underinvested in descriptive, comparative, and infrastructural questions.

#### Reveilhac (2024), *YouTube as an information source on politics and current affairs: Supply- and demand-side perspectives*
Reveilhac begins from the claim that YouTube is increasingly important as a news and public-affairs source, yet scholars still know relatively little about what users actually consume there. The paper frames the key unknowns as questions of genre and boundary: what kinds of alternative news channels exist, how they differ in style, strategy, and ideology, and how their audiences overlap. It also highlights uncertainty about the future relationship between creator-led political information and established news outlets. More broadly, it pushes the field toward supply-and-demand mapping of news YouTube rather than treating the platform simply as a container for misinformation or extremism.

#### Reynolds & Hallinan (2024), *User-generated accountability: Public participation in algorithmic governance on YouTube*
This article treats opacity as the central problem of YouTube governance. It argues that users and creators face automated and hybrid systems whose reasons, actors, and appeal processes are often unclear, making accountability difficult. The main research questions concern how users call platforms to account, which actors they target, and what forms of grievance emerge when responsibility is ambiguous. The key unknown is how meaningful accountability can develop when platform governance is opaque, distributed across humans and machines, and only weakly responsive to user complaints.

#### Lai, Brown, Bisbee, Tucker, Nagler, & Bonneau (2024), *Estimating the ideology of political YouTube videos*
Lai et al. frame measurement as a core bottleneck in political YouTube research. They argue that many studies rely on small bespoke samples and hand-labeled channel lists, which limits scale, replicability, and cross-study comparability. The principal unknown is how to estimate ideology at the video level across a much larger share of political YouTube, rather than inferring ideology indirectly from channels or external shares. The paper therefore points toward a larger unresolved agenda: building scalable measures of political content that are fine-grained enough to describe ideological supply, recommendation, and audience exposure over time.

#### Huang & Yang (2024), *Auditing entertainment traps on YouTube: How do recommendation algorithms pull users away from news?*
Huang and Yang argue that recommendation debates have focused too heavily on extremity and not enough on diversion. They identify a gap around what they call the entertainment trap: the possibility that recommendation systems steer users away from public-affairs content and toward more engaging but less civically useful entertainment. The main unknown is how strong this dynamic is and under what conditions it affects exposure to news. This shifts the literature from a narrow radicalization frame toward a broader democratic-information frame centered on opportunity costs and news avoidance.

#### Hosseinmardi et al. (2024), *Causally estimating the effect of YouTube's recommender system using counterfactual bots*
Hosseinmardi et al. diagnose a longstanding identification problem in recommender research. Observed viewing reflects both user preferences and algorithmic intervention, so standard observational studies struggle to isolate what the recommender itself is doing. Their key question is how to construct a plausible counterfactual for what a user would have watched absent recommendation. The article therefore frames one of the field's biggest unknowns as causal attribution: when problematic viewing occurs on YouTube, how much should be attributed to platform design versus user intention.

#### Hallinan, Reynolds, & Rothenstein (2024), *Copyright callouts and the promise of creator-driven platform governance*
This paper argues that creator complaints about copyright enforcement are not peripheral incidents but one of the main ways governance is publicly contested on YouTube. It frames the major unknowns as institutional and political rather than merely legal: how creators challenge enforcement decisions, when such challenges gain traction, and whether public callouts function as a meaningful governance mechanism in a platform environment with weak formal participation. The article extends the governance agenda by showing that monetization and copyright enforcement are central sites of YouTube power.

#### Chae & Lee (2024), *Where do cross-cutting discussions happen?*
Chae and Lee position comment sections as an underexamined corrective to strong echo-chamber claims. Their core question is where cross-cutting political discussion appears on YouTube and whether it differs between political vloggers and mainstream news outlets. The key unknown is not simply whether comments are polarized, but whether YouTube hosts spaces where users encounter and respond to opposing views. The article therefore keeps open the broader unresolved issue of whether YouTube should be understood as a segregated political environment or a mixed and situational one.

#### Violot, Elmas, Bilogrevic, & Humbert (2024), *Shorts vs. regular videos on YouTube: A comparative analysis of user engagement and content creation trends*
This paper frames format transition as a newly important and understudied question. As short-form video expands, the authors ask whether Shorts are displacing longer videos, how creators adapt their production strategies, and whether engagement metrics mean the same thing across formats. The article identifies two major unknowns: whether short-form growth changes the structure of attention on YouTube, and whether findings derived from regular videos can be generalized to Shorts. It is especially important for warning that format change may break older descriptive assumptions about views, engagement, and channel strategy.

#### Boesinger, Ribeiro, Veselovsky, & West (2024), *Tube2Vec: Social and semantic embeddings of YouTube channels*
Tube2Vec starts from the premise that YouTube researchers repeatedly confront a discovery and coding problem: relevant channels are hard to find, and manual annotation is expensive and low-recall. The paper's main contribution is infrastructural, but the unknown it surfaces is substantive as well: scholars still lack scalable ways to map the semantic and social neighborhoods of channels. By offering shared embeddings, it implicitly frames future YouTube research around better channel similarity, classification, and coverage.

### 2023

#### Rieder, Borra, Coromina, & Matamoros-Fernandez (2023), *Making a living in the creator economy: A large-scale study of linking on YouTube*
This paper argues that creator-economy research must move beyond monetization inside YouTube itself. Its central question is how creators use outbound links to build audiences, revenue streams, and dependencies across multiple platforms. The key unknowns concern the architecture of creator survival: how monetization strategies differ by category, geography, and channel size, and how much YouTube's unequal structure pushes creators toward off-platform income and networking. The article reframes YouTube not as a self-contained economy but as the hub of a wider monetization ecology.

#### Ormen & Gregersen (2023), *Institutional polymorphism: Diversification of content and monetization strategies on YouTube*
Ormen and Gregersen challenge the assumption that platformization produces straightforward convergence. They ask whether creators and media actors actually become more alike under platform pressures or instead diversify their content and monetization strategies to manage uncertainty and dependence. The main unknown is how organizational adaptation works on YouTube: whether channels converge on one dominant model or develop hybrid forms that combine multiple genres, revenue streams, and institutional logics. This is directly relevant to taxonomy work because it implies that rigid creator-versus-institution categories may miss the platform's hybrid forms.

#### Ormen & Gregersen (2023), *Towards the engagement economy: Interconnected processes of commodification on YouTube*
This article frames YouTube as part of a wider shift from attention markets to engagement-driven commodification. Its key question is how monetization, moderation, and platform strategy are being reorganized as platforms face competitive and regulatory pressure. The paper identifies a broader unknown about the changing political economy of YouTube: how creator labor, audience metrics, platform governance, and commercial incentives interact as the platform's business model evolves. It is useful as a statement that the creator economy cannot be studied separately from governance and monetization design.

#### McGrady, Zheng, Curran, Baumgartner, & Zuckerman (2023), *Dialing for videos: A random sample of YouTube*
This paper makes representativeness itself the major research problem. It argues that because YouTube is enormous, personalized, and hard to sample, many of its fundamental characteristics remain unknown. The central question is how to construct a more genuinely random view of the platform rather than relying on search results, recommendation trails, or elite-channel samples. The paper therefore identifies a foundational unknown underneath many substantive debates: without better sampling, researchers cannot say with confidence what ordinary YouTube looks like or how skewed prior samples have been.

#### Haroon, Wojcieszak, Chhabra, Liu, Mohapatra, & Shafiq (2023), *Auditing YouTube's recommendation system for ideologically congenial, extreme, and problematic recommendations*
Haroon et al. frame the recommender debate as empirically unsettled. Their key question is whether YouTube systematically recommends ideologically congenial, extremist, or otherwise problematic content, and to whom. The paper identifies the major unknown as heterogeneity: recommendation effects may differ by depth in the recommendation trail, by user starting point, and by content type. In doing so, it resists both blanket radicalization claims and blanket dismissals, and instead pushes the literature toward more conditional accounts of recommendation behavior.

#### Chen, Nyhan, Reifler, Robertson, & Wilson (2023), *Subscriptions and external links help drive resentful users to alternative and extremist YouTube channels*
Chen et al. argue that the strongest rabbit-hole claims overlook the role of prior demand and off-platform routing. Their main question is how viewers actually arrive at alternative and extremist YouTube content. The article identifies several key unknowns: how much exposure is concentrated among predisposed audiences, how much subscriptions matter, and how important external links are relative to platform recommendations. Its broader implication is that scholarship on harmful content must study YouTube together with adjacent infrastructures such as subscriptions, social sharing, and referral ecosystems.

#### Abou El-Komboz, Kerkhof, & Loh (2023), *Platform partnership programs and content supply: Evidence from the YouTube Adpocalypse*
This paper frames monetization thresholds and partnership eligibility as a central but understudied governance instrument. It asks how creators respond when access to the YouTube Partner Program is restricted and how those responses affect the amount, quality, and diversity of subsequent content. The unknown it emphasizes is how monetization governance reshapes supply, not just who gets paid. More generally, the paper argues that YouTube governance research should treat revenue eligibility rules as market-structuring interventions rather than background administrative details.

### 2022

#### Yesilada & Lewandowsky (2022), *Systematic review: YouTube recommendations and problematic content*
This systematic review frames the field as fragmented and methodologically constrained. It argues that limited access to the recommender system and incomplete knowledge of its actual operation make existing models of recommendation pathways imperfect. The key unknowns concern the size, mechanism, and consistency of recommendation effects on problematic content exposure. Its main contribution to the research agenda is cautionary: YouTube scholarship has strong claims and weak common measurement, so synthesis must be conditional and explicit about uncertainty.

#### Munger & Phillips (2022), *Right-wing YouTube: A supply and demand perspective*
Munger and Phillips treat the main gap as theoretical mis-specification. Instead of beginning with the recommender, they ask what technological affordances make YouTube distinctive and how the supply of and demand for right-wing video changed over time. The paper identifies two important unknowns: the platform-specific features that make YouTube attractive to political actors, and the extent to which growth in controversial content is driven by user demand rather than algorithmic push. It remains central because it shifts the agenda from one-dimensional algorithm stories toward a platform-political-economy framework.

#### Lee, Wu, Ertugrul, Lin, & Xie (2022), *Whose advantage? Measuring attention dynamics across YouTube and Twitter on controversial topics*
Lee et al. argue that prior work typically isolates either one platform or one issue, leaving cross-platform attention dynamics poorly understood. Their central question is how attention accumulates for ideological groups across YouTube and Twitter and across different controversial topics. The unknowns they emphasize are comparative and temporal: whether one side consistently gains more attention, whether those dynamics vary by issue, and how attention evolves over time. The article is useful for widening YouTube scholarship beyond platform-internal explanation toward comparative media-system analysis.

#### Brown, Bisbee, Lai, Bonneau, Nagler, & Tucker (2022), *Echo chambers, rabbit holes, and algorithmic bias: How YouTube recommends content to real users*
Brown et al. frame the rabbit-hole debate as a data problem as much as a substantive one. Their core question is how YouTube recommends content to real users rather than synthetic or highly stylized profiles. The article identifies a major unknown about the relationship between user history, platform-specific features, and recommendation outcomes, and it aims to reconcile conflicting prior findings by improving behavioral measurement. Its larger significance is that it makes clear how much the field's strongest claims have depended on limited or nonrepresentative observational designs.
