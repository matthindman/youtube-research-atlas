# YouTube Scholarship: Research Questions and Key Unknowns (2022–2026)

**Prepared for the YouTube Channel Census Project**
**Date:** March 2026

This report reviews 28 articles and reports published from 2022 to 2026, drawn from the YouTube paper archive. For each, it summarizes the paper's assessment of the most important research questions and key unknowns about YouTube. Papers are ordered from most recent to oldest. An overall synthesis follows.

---

## Overall Synthesis: The Landscape of YouTube Research

### The Platform We Don't Know

Across the 28 works reviewed here, a single theme dominates: **YouTube is the most important and least understood major platform.** Munger describes it as "the platform for which the ratio of academic attention to social and political importance seems to me to be the most out of whack" (Munger 2024, p. 2). The numbers are stark: "Only fifteen percent of users use Twitter, while over forty percent of papers study it. In contrast, YouTube is second only to Facebook in global usage at just over fifty percent, while receiving well under five percent of the attention" (Munger et al. 2025, p. 2). Political science has been especially negligent — the top three journals "have published a total of fifteen articles that are primarily based on the quantitative analysis of Twitter data (five each), but have never published an article primarily based on the quantitative analysis of YouTube data" (Munger and Phillips 2022, pp. 4–5). Norton and Shapiro quantify the disparity across the broader literature: "there were approximately 22.4 and 1.4 published papers per 100 million active users for Twitter and Facebook respectively," while "YouTube, WeChat, and Telegram attracted far less research despite their substantial userbases" (Norton and Shapiro 2024, as quoted in Munger et al. 2025, p. 2). Reveilhac puts it simply: "To date, scant scholarly attention has been directed toward YouTube as a news source" (Reveilhac 2024, p. 1).

### Six Core Research Gaps

The literature converges on six major clusters of unanswered questions:

**1. What is YouTube? The descriptive deficit.**

The most basic facts about YouTube remain unknown. As McGrady et al. write:

> "YouTube is one of the largest, most important communication platforms in the world, but while there is a great deal of research about the site, many of its fundamental characteristics remain unknown." (McGrady et al. 2023, p. 1)

> "Among these fundamental unknowns are the number of videos YouTube hosts, how many views those videos typically receive, how much the site as a whole has grown over time, and how frequently various languages are represented in videos. Missing data like this creates 'denominator problems' and 'distribution problems' which limit the kinds of claims we can make." (McGrady et al. 2023, p. 4)

This creates a severe problem for any research that purports to measure prevalence: "A study may identify a set of 500 YouTube channels which contained misinformation about the 2020 United States presidential election, but it would be limited in the claims it can make about the prevalence of this misinformation. In other words, 500 out of how many videos?" (McGrady et al. 2023, p. 4).

The platform itself provides almost nothing to work with. "YouTube does not release most basic information about what it contains, and the company does not provide a mechanism to produce random samples" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 2). Even language — a basic descriptive dimension — is opaque: "YouTube does not share the distribution of languages spoken on its site with the public. A language code is not automatically assigned to a video, so it is not something easily calculable based on metadata" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 3). Kate Starbird has called YouTube "'almost inscrutable' compared to other platforms like Twitter, which historically made it possible to study a representative subset of posts" (as quoted in McGrady et al. 2025, p. 2).

Existing research methods compound the problem. Most studies begin from biased starting points: "most studies of YouTube either begin with a list of known channels or videos or create opportunistic samples by collecting videos recommended by YouTube's algorithms. ... videos collected via recommendation are more likely to be popular than purely random videos, both because YouTube promotes them and because a human or algorithm determined they were worth recommending" (McGrady et al. 2023, p. 3). For outsiders, "there is no way ... to draw a truly random sample of YouTube's channel population" (Ørmen and Gregersen 2023, pp. 436–437), and even the platform's own categories are unreliable: "Official content categories provided by YouTube are both vague (e.g., 'People and Blogs') and incomplete (e.g., excluding categories for children's content). Furthermore, in the current instantiation of the video upload process, 'People and Blogs' is the default choice" (Ørmen and Gregersen 2023, p. 437). Random sampling has "only recently become accessible to researchers, leaving large gaps in comparative research" (McGrady et al. 2025, pp. 2–3).

Munger argues that this descriptive deficit has distorted the entire research agenda: "The academic agenda has yet to adapt to the reality that the majority of time spent on social media is spent on non-textual platforms. For a variety of reasons, some legitimate others merely omphalocentric, we spend far too much time studying Facebook and especially Twitter" (Munger 2024, p. 9). His prescription: "qualitative description must be complemented by rigorous quantitative description as the second stage of the academic research process. ... I argue that we are still allocating insufficient efforts to qualitative and quantitative description" (Munger 2024, p. 24).

**2. Recommendation algorithms: radicalization, filter bubbles, and the demand-side challenge.**

The question that has dominated the literature — whether YouTube's recommendation algorithm radicalizes users — is now widely recognized as poorly specified. Munger is blunt: "Using a variety of different approaches, none of the academic research on the role of the YouTube algorithm in the promotion of extremist videos finds anything remotely like the provocative 'rabbit hole' hypothesis" (Munger 2024, p. 14). He argues the hypothesis was always theoretically implausible — "a model of YouTube media effects that centers the recommendation engine is implausible, an unfortunate update of the 'hypodermic needle' model" (Munger 2024, p. 13) — and attributes its popularity to wishful thinking: "it implies an obvious policy solution — one which is flattering to the journalists and academics studying the phenomenon" (Munger 2024, p. 14). Haroon et al. concur that the evidence base is weak: "Algorithms of social media platforms are often criticized for recommending ideologically congenial and radical content to their users. Despite these concerns, evidence on such filter bubbles and rabbit holes of radicalization is inconclusive" (Haroon et al. 2023, p. 1).

Multiple experimental and quasi-experimental studies now find limited or null short-term effects. Liu et al., in the largest such study, report that "manipulating algorithmic recommendations to create these conditions has limited effects on opinions ... even heavy-handed (although short-term) perturbations of real-world recommendations have limited causal effects on policy attitudes" (Liu et al. 2025, p. 1). More specifically: "in the most credible study of algorithmic polarization to date, we observe only minimal attitudinal shifts as a result of more extreme recommendations, calling into question widely circulating, unequivocal claims about their influence on political polarization. We are not claiming that polarization from recommendation systems cannot be found anywhere, but the consistent lack of short-term effects suggests that it is not everywhere" (Liu et al. 2025, p. 2). Brown et al. similarly "do not find evidence that many go down 'rabbit holes' that lead them to ideologically extreme content" (Brown et al. 2022, p. 1), and Yu et al. find that "increased news consumption has no effects on a range of survey outcomes (i.e. political participation, belief accuracy, perceived and affective polarization, and support for democratic norms), adding to the growing evidence of limited attitudinal effects of on-platform exposure" (Yu et al. 2024, p. 1).

User preferences, not algorithmic supply, appear to be the primary driver. Hosseinmardi et al. find that "on average, relying exclusively on the YouTube recommender results in less partisan consumption, where the effect is most pronounced for heavy partisan consumers ... individual consumption patterns mostly reflect individual preferences, where algorithmic recommendations play, if anything, a moderating role" (Hosseinmardi et al. 2024, p. 1). Chen et al. report that "exposure to alternative and extremist channel videos on YouTube is heavily concentrated among a small group of people with high prior levels of gender and racial resentment. These viewers often subscribe to these channels (prompting recommendations to their videos) and follow external links to them. In contrast, nonsubscribers rarely see or follow recommendations to videos from these channels" (Chen et al. 2023, p. 1) — indeed, "only 3% of participants who were not already subscribed to alternative or extremist channels viewed a video from one of these channels based on a recommendation" (Chen et al. 2023, p. 2). Hosseinmardi et al. draw the broader conclusion: "popular narratives about the widespread and profound manipulative impact of algorithms are overstated. This is not to say that highly problematic content does not exist on social media platforms ... Rather, by shifting the emphasis of the concern from presumed biases in algorithms to the factors governing the supply and demand of problematic content, social media companies and their critics can more accurately target the source of the problem" (Hosseinmardi et al. 2024, p. 6). Munger argues the real threat is not algorithmic but social: "the true threat posed by some right-wing content on YouTube is the capacity for creators to draw communities of committed viewers that mutually create and reinforce radical political canons, including some that promote hatred" (Munger and Phillips 2022, p. 3).

Three important caveats recur. On long-term effects: "We emphasize that this evidence does not rule out the possibility that YouTube is a radicalizing force in American politics because our design does not address long-term exposure or potential effects in particularly susceptible subpopulations" (Liu et al. 2025, p. 2). On vulnerable subpopulations: "We cannot rule out the existence of a small — but highly susceptible — population that cannot be detected with our sample sizes" (Liu et al. 2025, pp. 9–10). On temporal specificity: "in 2019 YouTube implemented significant changes to its algorithm that it claimed reduced watch time of 'borderline content and harmful misinformation' by 50 to 70%. ... our findings should be interpreted as applying only to the post-2019 period" (Hosseinmardi et al. 2024, p. 6). Chen et al. note that "even low levels of algorithmic amplification can have damaging consequences when extrapolated over YouTube's vast user base and across time. Further, it may be the case that the susceptible population was already radicalized during YouTube's pre-2019 era" (Chen et al. 2023, p. 2). And Yesilada and Lewandowsky highlight the fundamental opacity problem: "due to limited access and an incomplete understanding of the YouTube recommender system, the models built by researchers might not reflect the actual mechanisms underlying the YouTube recommender system and pathways to problematic content" (Yesilada and Lewandowsky 2022, p. 1).

The field is converging on a view that the more consequential algorithmic effect may be "interest bias" rather than ideological radicalization. Yu et al. explain: "because most users go to platforms for entertainment, not for verified news and quality public affairs, and because many engage with low-quality, sensationalist, and click-baity content, algorithmic recommendations align with these interests and preferences. This, combined with the fact that algorithms actively direct users to entertainment and away from news, leads to closed loops of low-quality, nonnews content consumption" (Yu et al. 2024, p. 2). Their experiment found that "nudging the algorithm significantly and sustainably increases both recommendations to and consumption of news and also minimizes ideological biases in recommendations and consumption ... In contrast, nudging the users has no observable effects on news consumption" (Yu et al. 2024, p. 1) — suggesting the algorithm is a more powerful lever than user motivation, but for content-type exposure rather than ideological direction.

**3. The creator economy: inequality, precarity, and institutional form.**

YouTube's creator economy is characterized by extreme concentration. Rieder et al. find that "this ecosystem is highly unequal — we found that the top 0.4% of channels account for 62% of views" (Rieder et al. 2023, p. 2). Verwiebe et al. document that among monetized German-speaking channels, "with minimum average monthly earnings of $0.02 and a maximum of $724,868, this right-skewed distribution translates into a Gini coefficient of 0.89 for the full sample and 0.95 for the random sample. Thus, earnings on YouTube are remarkably unequal, even more than in (most) non-digital labor markets" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, p. 12). At the top, "the 'winners' in the top 1% (N = 225) ... accumulate about 46% of the total earnings distributed among the CCs and earn $37,011 per month" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, p. 13).

The precarity facing most creators is deep. Rieder et al. describe "the vast majority of creators ... stuck on the lower rungs of what we call the 'aspirational curve,' that is, the emergent distribution of success in a market where large numbers of participants are willing to perform 'hope labor,' a 'future-oriented investment' (Kuehn & Corrigan, 2013, p. 10) that is likely to never pan out. Our numbers indeed show a steep income pyramid where the lower tiers are basically working for free" (Rieder et al. 2023, p. 4). The system sustains itself because "a system animated by low barriers of entry, high aspirational attractivity, performance-based pay, and hierarchization through cumulative advantage (Hindman, 2009) is bound to produce a long-tailed income distribution where the vast majority of participants will not be able to find economic success" (Rieder et al. 2023, p. 4). Platform pressures intensify this: "bans, demonetization, and algorithmic invisibility are constant threats ... particularly for channels that deal with sensitive topics" (Rieder et al. 2023, p. 3).

We lack systematic knowledge about the sociostructural dimensions of this inequality. Verwiebe et al. note that "surprisingly enough, there is a lack of comprehensive quantitative studies exploring earnings on YouTube that look beyond platform-related characteristics and consider conventional mechanisms of inequality, such as age, education, gender, and race" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, p. 2), and more pointedly: "relatively little is known about sociostructural variables, such as age, educational background, gender, or race. To our knowledge, no quantitative study deals with how individual characteristics influence inequality in earnings on YouTube" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, pp. 8–9).

Whether the creator economy follows winner-take-all or long-tail dynamics — or both — remains unresolved. Verwiebe et al. note that "research proposes two distinct frameworks for theorizing earning inequalities on digital platforms: the long-tail theory and winner-take-all markets" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, p. 3), and conclude that YouTube resembles "a winner-take-all and superstar market ... While it offers more monetization options than some other social media platforms, unstable and low earnings show the substantial difficulties of making a living as a CC" (Verwiebe et al. 2025, p. 20).

The institutional form of channels — independent creator, legacy media, state broadcaster, brand — is critical but underoperationalized. Munger's empirical strategy is "to classify a large sample of political YouTube channels as either affiliated with some larger media entity or as independent ... this distinction between affiliated channels and independent YouTubers results in significant system-level differences in a variety of platform-defined audience metrics" (Munger 2024, p. 1). Zaitsev and Clark find that "large media outlets tend to dominate left-leaning content, while right-leaning content is primarily produced by independent creators" (Zaitsev and Clark 2025, p. 1). Yet no existing taxonomy covers the full universe: "YouTube is one of the most influential platforms for news and political commentary; however, its vast scale makes it challenging to study the full range of content and communities that it hosts. Most existing research focuses only on the most popular channels, overlooking the large number of smaller creators who may reach sizable and politically engaged audiences" (Zaitsev and Clark 2025, p. 1). As a result, "prior studies have significantly underestimated the prevalence of right-leaning and conspiracy-themed content, which is more prevalent among smaller channels" (Zaitsev and Clark 2025, p. 1).

**4. YouTube as a news ecosystem.**

YouTube is now a top-tier news source globally. The Reuters Digital News Report 2025 finds that "over half of our samples in India, Thailand, and Kenya say they use YouTube for news" (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 16), with "top markets ... almost all in the Global South" (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 16). In the United States, "the proportion accessing news via social media and video networks (54%) is sharply up — overtaking both TV news (50%) and news websites/apps (48%) for the first time" (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 11). The UK's Ofcom report identifies YouTube as one of the "top four individual news sources" for UK adults (Ofcom 2025, p. 6), while Pew establishes YouTube as the most widely used platform overall — "the vast majority of U.S. adults (84%) say they ever use YouTube" with "about half" visiting daily (Pew 2025, pp. 4, 9).

But the character of this news ecosystem is poorly understood. Reveilhac observes: "To date, scant scholarly attention has been directed toward YouTube as a news source. While opinion surveys offer insights into the extent of YouTube consumption, our comprehension of the actual news content people consume, especially in terms of opinionated information created by opinion YouTubers and alternative channels covering news about politics and current affairs, remains limited" (Reveilhac 2024, p. 1). Munger frames this as a structural failure: "YouTube, the platform for which the ratio of academic attention to social and political importance seems to me to be the most out of whack" (Munger 2024, p. 6).

Political commentary dominates the space and is overwhelmingly male. Newman et al. report: "Commentary is the most frequently mentioned category represented in our data and generally describes online political chat show hosts (such as Tucker Carlson, Joe Rogan, and Russell Brand). Commentary tends to be cheap and does not require investment in newsgathering or infrastructure" (Newman et al. 2025, p. 10). Across 24 countries, "the vast majority (85%) of the top 15 in each country are ... men" (Newman et al. 2025, p. 14). The boundary between journalism and other content forms is "extremely fuzzy" — "the dividing lines between these two categories (and their respective sub-categories) can be extremely fuzzy" (Newman et al. 2025, p. 9). Reveilhac concurs: "Although traditional media retains influence due to its extensive reach, credibility, and commitment to journalistic standards, the line between traditional news channels and alternative news channels is blurring" (Reveilhac 2024, p. 1).

Right-leaning US commentators are being exported globally via YouTube. Newman et al. find that "in most countries attention is focused on domestic creators, but many English-speaking countries show a different pattern. In particular, right-leaning political commentators from the United States, such as Joe Rogan, Tucker Carlson, and Ben Shapiro, have found fertile ground for their pro-free speech and anti-mainstream media narratives in a number of countries ... Two-thirds of the most mentioned list in Canada are creators/personalities based in the United States or the UK, with an even higher number seen in Australia" (Newman et al. 2025, p. 13).

The displacement of institutional journalism is accelerating: "An accelerating shift towards consumption via social media and video platforms is further diminishing the influence of 'institutional journalism' and supercharging a fragmented alternative media environment containing an array of podcasters, YouTubers, and TikTokers. Populist politicians around the world are increasingly able to bypass traditional journalism in favour of friendly partisan media, 'personalities', and 'influencers' who often get special access but rarely ask difficult questions, with many implicated in spreading false narratives or worse" (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 10). And audiences are aware of the problem: "online influencers and personalities are seen as the biggest threat worldwide (47%), along with national politicians (47%)" as sources of false or misleading information (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 11).

**5. Cross-linguistic and cross-national variation.**

YouTube operates in virtually every language and country, but research is overwhelmingly Anglophone and US-focused. McGrady et al. note that "existing research has found ... that most literature on social media use and wellbeing is based on English samples ... most studies of Wikipedia are based on the English Wikipedia ... and most research about Twitter focuses on English, with few studies attending to its international popularity" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 1). YouTube is no exception: "Research on YouTube in particular and social media in general is skewed toward English both in terms of samples and publications, with most research about YouTube published in the US and concentrated in a relatively small number of journals" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 3). Norton and Shapiro find that over 65% of papers study only Western democracies, with more than half focused exclusively on the United States (Norton and Shapiro 2024, as quoted in Munger et al. 2025, p. 2).

Cross-linguistic comparison reveals dramatic differences. Hindi YouTube stands out: "The most salient contrast, however, is between Hindi YouTube and the other three languages. Hindi videos are much shorter and much newer, with sharp growth since 2020 and more than half of the sample uploaded in 2023 alone" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 1). The numbers are striking: "The duration of each video in seconds ... the median of the four languages shows one of the most significant differences in our analysis: 119, 148, 29, and 98 seconds for English, Spanish, Hindi, and Russian, respectively" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 11). "More than half of Hindi videos are 30 seconds or less, and there are very few long-form videos longer than 20 minutes (2.51%)" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 11).

This likely reflects the migration of Indian short-form video culture from TikTok: "Prior to the ban, YouTube was not among the most popular platforms in India, but it appears that the culture of short-form video creation cultivated by TikTok migrated over to YouTube when TikTok was no longer available. Another of TikTok's fingerprints is visible in the number of videos in all languages — but especially Hindi — which are exactly 15 seconds long, a long-time TikTok default" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 17). Russian YouTube operates under distinct constraints: "Amid heavy state censorship of media, YouTube remains one of the primary ways citizens access unfiltered news. Even after throttling YouTube in the summer of 2024, many people still access the site directly or through VPNs" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 3).

These differences mean that findings from English-language studies cannot be assumed to generalize: "Received wisdom about large American social media platforms like YouTube might lead us to believe that the site is skewed toward English-language content. Previous research has shown that the perceived dominance of English on the internet broadly is frequently overstated" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 16). The Reuters Digital News Report confirms that YouTube's role varies enormously across markets: "YouTube content in India has been exploding in recent years with a host of successful talk shows that are both critical of and supportive of the government of Narendra Modi" (Reuters DNR 2025, p. 17). Whether YouTube is one platform or many remains an open question. McGrady et al. frame their work as an effort "to highlight heterogeneity where it is tempting to assume similarity" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 4).

**6. Platform governance, content moderation, and data access.**

Content moderation on YouTube is opaque by design. Hallinan et al. observe that "as platforms have scaled up, so too has content moderation, bringing together users, contracted labourers, and algorithmic systems into centralised and opaque arrangements" (Hallinan et al. 2025, p. 5). Academic and industry efforts to assess whether platforms are biased have produced no resolution: "Academic and industry research offers ultimately inconclusive accounts of the presence and direction of platform biases, stymied by the emergent dynamics of complex socio-technical systems and restricted access to relevant data" (Hallinan et al. 2025, p. 3). Yesilada and Lewandowsky make the same point from the algorithmic side: "due to limited access and an incomplete understanding of the YouTube recommender system, the models built by researchers might not reflect the actual mechanisms underlying the YouTube recommender system and pathways to problematic content" (Yesilada and Lewandowsky 2022, p. 1).

Creators across the political spectrum report feeling targeted, but these complaints are internally contradictory. Hallinan et al. find that "ideological suspicion, or 'the assumption that platforms are biased against one's personal beliefs' (Riedl et al., 2023, p. 2164), manifests across the political spectrum" (Hallinan et al. 2025, p. 5). Yet "these accounts suggest that the growing consensus around platform bias contains significant internal contradictions. Yet we know little about how these competing claims play out in practice" (Hallinan et al. 2025, p. 5).

The EU's Digital Services Act is creating new data access mechanisms, but they are untested. The Commission announced that "as of 29 October 2025, new rules under the Digital Services Act (DSA) will allow researchers to gain unprecedented access to very large online platforms' data to study the societal impact stemming from the platforms' systems" (European Commission 2025). But the process requires researchers to "undergo a strict assessment carried out by Digital Services Coordinators" (European Commission 2025), and the uniformity of that assessment process does not yet exist.

Meanwhile, API restrictions have dramatically curtailed independent research capacity. Munger et al. report: "The API uses a quota system in which different calls cost different amounts of quota. Up until the end of 2019, free public API keys had a maximum limit of 1,000,000 points daily. Changes to the API dramatically reduced this amount to 10,000 daily points" (Munger et al. 2025, p. 8). Munger elaborates: "In the summer of 2019, YouTube changed the base limit for new API accounts to ten thousand per day — a 99 percent decrease. My API key's limit was grandfathered in, however, and I can still (as of March 2023) collect two orders of magnitude more data per day than new accounts can. ... A project of this scale would not be tractable with the ten thousand unit rate limit" (Munger 2024, p. 52).

The rise of YouTube Shorts is reshaping content production but is critically understudied. Violot et al. note: "Short-form videos are currently understudied and large-scale analyses of such data are relatively uncommon. This is first because the short-form video concept has gained widespread popularity and became the primary feature of social media platforms only recently, e.g., YouTube introduced Shorts and their section in 2021. Second, the platforms have been restrictive in their data-sharing policies" (Violot et al. 2024, p. 222). Their paper is "the first comparative analysis of YouTube Shorts versus regular videos with respect to user engagement (i.e., views, likes, and comments), content creation frequency and video categories" (Violot et al. 2024, p. 213).

### Implications for the YouTube Census Project

The literature reviewed here provides strong justification for the Census project's core design choices:

- **The descriptive deficit is real and widely recognized.** Munger argues that "qualitative description must be complemented by rigorous quantitative description as the second stage of the academic research process. ... I argue that we are still allocating insufficient efforts to qualitative and quantitative description" (Munger 2024, p. 24). Munger et al. frame their own work as answering Gerring's "call for research that consists of 'mere description'" (Munger et al. 2025, p. 2). McGrady et al. write that "YouTube is an important, immensely popular, and critically understudied platform in part due to the difficulties it poses to researchers" (McGrady et al. 2023, p. 5).
- **Institutional-form taxonomy is the critical gap.** Munger's empirical strategy is "to classify a large sample of political YouTube channels as either affiliated with some larger media entity or as independent" and demonstrates that "this distinction ... results in significant system-level differences in a variety of platform-defined audience metrics" (Munger 2024, p. 1). Newman et al. acknowledge the difficulty: "in practice the dividing lines are extremely fuzzy. Many creators defy categorisation" (Newman et al. 2025, p. 10). No existing dataset covers the full channel universe with validated institutional-form labels.
- **Cross-linguistic coverage is essential.** McGrady et al. frame their work as a "counterpoint to assumptions that popular or English-language content is representative of a platform as a whole" (McGrady et al. 2025, p. 3).
- **Concentration and inequality metrics are novel for YouTube.** While Gini coefficients and rank-size distributions have been applied to small YouTube samples, no study has applied them to a near-census of the 10k+ subscriber universe — the scale at which these measures become meaningful.
- **Longitudinal panel data is the frontier.** The mobility, precarity, and contestability questions in Paper 2 address topics that the literature identifies as important but has never been able to study at scale due to the absence of sustained, large-panel viewership data.

## 2026

### Verwiebe, Osorio-Krauter, Fritsch, Weissmann, Philipp, and Buder (2025) — Unequal Earnings on YouTube

**Full citation:** Verwiebe, Roland; Chiara Osorio-Krauter; Nina-Sophie Fritsch; Sarah Weissmann; Aaron Philipp; and Claudia Buder. 2025. "Working on an algorithm-controlled platform as a content creator: what explains unequal earnings on YouTube?" *European Societies*. https://doi.org/10.1162/EUSO.a.42

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper identifies a major gap in understanding earnings inequality among YouTube content creators. While prior research has examined alternative monetization strategies (affiliate links, merchandise, sponsorships) and platform-specific variables like subscriber counts and content categories, the authors argue that virtually no large-scale quantitative research has examined how conventional sociostructural factors — gender, age, race, and education — drive earnings inequality on YouTube. The paper finds extreme inequality (Gini coefficient of 0.89) among monetized German-speaking channels, consistent with winner-take-all dynamics. A key unknown is whether YouTube's creator economy follows the "long tail" theory, where niche creators can find viable audiences, or a winner-take-all market where small differences in talent or visibility produce vastly disproportionate rewards. The authors note that these two dynamics may coexist, with Pareto distributions at the top and a long heavy tail below. The paper also highlights that YouTube's algorithmic infrastructure may itself reproduce social inequalities — rewarding or penalizing creators based on race, gender, and demographics in opaque ways — but the mechanisms remain poorly understood. The lack of available RPM (revenue per mille) data from YouTube makes it difficult to study earnings directly, forcing researchers to rely on estimates. The paper calls for further investigation into how platform algorithms interact with sociostructural characteristics to produce and sustain economic stratification among creators.

## 2025

### Munger, Bisbee, Yalcin, Phillips, and Hindman (2025) — Pressing Play on Politics

**Full citation:** Munger, Kevin; James Bisbee; Omer Yalcin; Joseph Phillips; and Matthew Hindman. 2025. "Pressing Play on Politics: Quantitative Description of YouTube." *Journal of Quantitative Description: Digital Media* 5(2025): 1–37. https://doi.org/10.51685/jqd.2025.006

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper's central concern is that YouTube is drastically understudied relative to its enormous user base. While YouTube is the most-used social media platform in the United States with 2.3 billion monthly active users, it receives under 5% of academic attention compared to Twitter, which has a fraction of the users but attracts over 40% of research papers. The authors argue that without adequate descriptive knowledge of what YouTube is, it is impossible to know whether causal hypotheses about the platform are important. The paper identifies several key unknowns. First, participatory inequality: just 2% of commenters write 50% of all comments on political channels, and these super-users are more toxic than typical commenters, raising questions about whether YouTube comments represent genuine public opinion or a vocal, unrepresentative minority. Second, the platform is evolving rapidly — COVID-era content moderation, the migration of far-right creators to alternative platforms like Rumble and BitChute, and the rise of progressive "BreadTube" creators have fundamentally reshaped political YouTube, but research has not kept pace with these changes. Third, the paper highlights that API restrictions (reduced from 1,000,000 to 10,000 daily quota points) severely limit researchers' ability to study the platform at scale. Fourth, ideological network structure is poorly mapped: comment patterns reveal clear partisan divides, yet a few bridging channels (e.g., Joe Rogan) connect otherwise insular communities in ways that are not well understood.

### Liu, Hu, Savas, Baum, Berinsky, Chaney, Lucas, Mariman, de Benedictis-Kessner, Guess, Knox, and Stewart (2025) — Short-term Exposure to Filter Bubbles

**Full citation:** Liu, Naijia; Xinlan Emily Hu; Yasemin Savas; Matthew A. Baum; Adam J. Berinsky; Allison J. B. Chaney; Christopher Lucas; Rei Mariman; Justin de Benedictis-Kessner; Andrew M. Guess; Dean Knox; and Brandon M. Stewart. 2025. "Short-term exposure to filter-bubble recommendation systems has limited polarization effects: Naturalistic experiments on YouTube." *PNAS* 122(8): e2318127122. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2318127122

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper directly challenges the prevailing narrative that YouTube's recommendation algorithm radicalizes users through filter bubbles and rabbit holes. The paper frames YouTube as a "platformed media system" characterized by extreme inequality — the top 0.4% of channels account for 62% of views — and asks how this structural inequality shapes creator strategies. Major gaps the paper identifies include: how monetization and networking strategies vary across channel sizes, content categories, and geographic locations; what role external revenue sources (affiliate marketing, crowdfunding, sponsorships) play relative to YouTube's native advertising revenue share; and how these strategies have evolved over time as the platform has changed its policies. The authors note that YouTube's creator economy is notoriously hard to scope because much economic activity occurs off-platform or through informal arrangements. The paper emphasizes that YouTube exerts significant economic pressure on creators through demonetization, algorithmic invisibility, and policy changes, yet we lack systematic knowledge about how creators respond to this precarity. Creators operate in a landscape of constant uncertainty due to evolving platform rules, pay rates, and intermediaries. The paper reveals that linking to external monetization and social networking has become more prevalent over time but differs substantially by channel tier, underscoring that the creator economy's benefits are unevenly distributed in ways that remain poorly documented at scale.

### McGrady, Zheng, Curran, Baumgartner, and Zuckerman (2023) — Dialing for Videos

**Full citation:** McGrady, R., Zheng, K., Curran, R., Baumgartner, J., & Zuckerman, E. 2023. "Dialing for Videos: A Random Sample of YouTube." *Journal of Quantitative Description: Digital Media* 3: 1–85.

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper argues that many of YouTube's most fundamental characteristics remain unknown, creating pervasive "denominator problems" and "distribution problems" that severely limit the claims researchers can make. The paper identifies two root causes: the difficulty of studying audiovisual content at scale (unlike text on Twitter or Facebook, video resists computational analysis), and the opacity of YouTube's proprietary algorithms and practices. Key unknowns the paper highlights include: how many videos YouTube hosts in total, how views are distributed across the platform, how much the site has grown over time, what languages are represented, and what proportion of content falls into various categories. Without this baseline information, studies of harmful content or misinformation on YouTube cannot establish prevalence — for example, identifying 500 channels with election misinformation is meaningless without knowing "500 out of how many?" The paper notes that YouTube provides no mechanism for obtaining a random sample of its videos, so most studies begin with known channels or collect videos through recommendations, producing samples that are systematically biased toward more popular content. The authors present a novel brute-force sampling method to address these gaps. Their findings reveal YouTube as far more linguistically and culturally diverse than any single user would experience, with most videos receiving very few views. The paper establishes critical baselines — such as YouTube's estimated total size and growth trajectory — that provide the contextual foundation other researchers need to make meaningful claims about the platform as a whole.

### Munger and Phillips (2022) — Right-Wing YouTube

**Full citation:** Munger, K. & Phillips, J. 2022. "Right-wing YouTube: A supply and demand perspective." *International Journal of Press/Politics* 27(1): 186–219.

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper argues that YouTube is dramatically understudied in political science despite being the most-used social network in the United States and the only major platform more popular among right-leaning users. The authors identify a striking disciplinary gap: the top three political science journals have never published an article based on quantitative analysis of YouTube data, despite publishing fifteen based on Twitter data. The paper proposes a "supply and demand" framework to address two core research questions: what technological affordances make YouTube distinct from other platforms and distinctly popular among the online right, and how the supply of and demand for right-wing content have changed over time. Key unknowns include the relative roles of algorithmic recommendation versus user choice in driving viewership of extreme content, and whether the "radicalization pipeline" narrative is supported by systematic evidence or rests primarily on journalistic accounts biased toward sensationalism. The authors argue that the true threat from right-wing YouTube is not "radicalization by algorithm" but rather the platform's capacity for creators to build committed viewer communities that mutually create and reinforce radical political canons. The paper highlights that YouTube's combination of low content-creation costs, automated audience matching through recommendations, monetization opportunities, and the immersive parasocial capacities of video make it uniquely important. Their descriptive analysis reveals that far-right viewership peaked in 2017 — well before YouTube's 2019 algorithm changes — while mainstream conservative content has grown substantially, suggesting that demand-side dynamics may matter more than algorithmic supply in explaining patterns of political media consumption on the platform.

### Yesilada and Lewandowsky (2022) — Systematic Review of YouTube Recommendations and Problematic Content

**Full citation:** Yesilada, M. & Lewandowsky, S. 2022. "Systematic review: YouTube recommendations and problematic content." *Internet Policy Review* 11(1).

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This systematic review asks whether the YouTube recommender system facilitates pathways to problematic content such as extremist, radicalizing, conspiratorial, and misinformative material. After assessing 1,187 studies and filtering to 23 that met eligibility criteria, the review finds that 14 studies implicated the recommender system in facilitating problematic content pathways, seven produced mixed results, and only two did not implicate the system. However, the authors identify a fundamental epistemological limitation: due to limited access to YouTube's proprietary systems and an incomplete understanding of how the recommender system actually works, the models built by researchers may not reflect the actual mechanisms underlying YouTube's recommendations. Key unknowns include the balance between users actively seeking out problematic content versus the recommender system leading individuals to content they would not otherwise have encountered. The review highlights that YouTube's autoplay feature, which automatically plays recommended videos, complicates the question of user agency. The authors note tensions in the literature between studies finding filter bubbles and those challenging the concept, and between evidence that social media causes negative political attitudes and findings that effects are more nuanced. Although YouTube itself has taken remedial steps — removing millions of violating videos and claiming to reduce borderline content — the success of these measures remains uncertain. The review underscores that the field lacks a consistent definition of "problematic content," that studies use widely varying methodologies making comparison difficult, and that the dynamic nature of YouTube's algorithm (which changes over time, often without disclosure) means that findings may become outdated quickly.

### Ørmen and Gregersen (2022) — Institutional Polymorphism on YouTube

**Full citation:** Ørmen, J. & Gregersen, A. 2023. "Institutional polymorphism: Diversification of content and monetization strategies on YouTube." *Television & New Media* 24(4): 432–451.

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper examines whether YouTube's governance systems — its guidelines, terms of service, algorithmic curation, and tiered monetization structure — lead to isomorphism (uniformity) or polymorphism (diversification) in how creators manage their presence on the platform. The key research questions are: to what extent do YouTube channels converge on specific content niches, how and to what extent are they monetizing their presence, and how are differences in content and monetization strategies manifested in distinct organizational identities? The paper identifies important gaps in understanding platform power: scholars have argued that YouTube's institutional pressures (demonetization threats, algorithmic invisibility, Partner Program requirements, advertiser-friendliness demands) should push creators toward conformity, but this assumption has not been empirically tested with a broad sample. The authors note significant methodological unknowns: there is no way for outsiders to draw a truly random sample of YouTube's channel population, the platform's content categories are vague and potentially invalid (e.g., "People & Blogs" is a default category that may not reflect deliberate choices), and key features like monetization strategies cannot be searched through the API. Their findings support diversification rather than convergence, suggesting that creators adapt to, negotiate with, and defy institutional pressures in varied ways — complicating the dominant narrative that platforms exercise homogenizing power over cultural production. The paper argues that institutional platform studies have been too focused on isomorphism and need to better account for creator agency and the range of strategic responses to platform governance.

### Lai, Brown, Bisbee, Tucker, Nagler, and Bonneau (2022) — Estimating the Ideology of Political YouTube Videos

**Full citation:** Lai, A., Brown, M. A., Bisbee, J., Tucker, J. A., Nagler, J., & Bonneau, R. 2024. "Estimating the ideology of political YouTube videos." *Political Analysis* 32: 345–360.

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper addresses a fundamental methodological gap in YouTube research: the absence of a scalable, validated method for estimating the ideological positioning of individual YouTube videos. The authors note that existing research has relied on bespoke datasets of a few thousand channels labeled by humans, with ideological labels applied only at the channel level — precluding analysis of individual videos or within-channel variation. These datasets are inherently static and require further human labeling for each update. The key research question is whether cross-platform behavioral data (specifically, patterns of YouTube video sharing on ideologically distinct Reddit communities) can be leveraged to estimate video-level ideology at scale. Key unknowns the paper highlights include: the prevalence of ideological echo chambers in individuals' YouTube viewing habits, the relationship between a video's ideological extremism and its engagement metrics, and how the ideological landscape of YouTube content changes over time. The paper presents a two-step method using correspondence analysis on Reddit-YouTube linking patterns followed by a text-based language model, producing ideology estimates that are validated against human labels. The authors demonstrate that individuals' watch histories do favor ideologically congruent content, and that ideologically extreme videos may receive greater engagement — though they note these findings require further investigation. The paper fills a critical infrastructure gap for the field: without reliable, scalable ideology measures, researchers cannot rigorously test claims about filter bubbles, radicalization, or algorithmic bias on YouTube.

### Brown, Bisbee, Lai, Bonneau, Nagler, and Tucker (2022) — Echo Chambers, Rabbit Holes, and Algorithmic Bias

**Full citation:** Brown, M. A., Bisbee, J., Lai, A., Bonneau, R., Nagler, J., & Tucker, J. A. 2022. "Echo chambers, rabbit holes, and algorithmic bias: How YouTube recommends content to real users." SSRN Working Paper No. 4114905. Center for Social Media and Politics, New York University.

**Research questions and key unknowns about YouTube:**
This paper identifies three theoretically distinct but frequently conflated phenomena as the core unknowns about YouTube's information environment: ideological echo chambers (whether users receive ideologically homogeneous content centered on their own views), extremist rabbit holes (whether the recommendation algorithm progressively pushes users toward more extreme content over successive recommendations), and system-wide ideological bias (whether the platform systematically nudges all users in one ideological direction). The authors argue that prior research has been hampered by a fundamental data limitation: studies using real user watch histories cannot disentangle the algorithm's influence from user choice, while studies using anonymous scraping disconnect the recommendation algorithm from the user behavioral data it needs to operate, undermining construct validity. The paper's experimental design — surveying real YouTube users and randomly assigning some to follow recommendations while others choose freely — aims to isolate the algorithm's causal effect. The findings reveal only mild echo chambers, little evidence of rabbit holes leading to ideologically extreme content, and a moderate system-wide bias toward conservative content (though toward the moderate center, not the extremes). The authors emphasize that their results do not mean YouTube lacks extremist content, but rather that the recommendation algorithm may not be the primary avenue by which users encounter it — suggesting that search functions, subscriptions, and external links deserve more scrutiny. The paper highlights that the popular press has failed to distinguish between these three concepts, leading to confused public discourse about what YouTube's algorithm actually does.
