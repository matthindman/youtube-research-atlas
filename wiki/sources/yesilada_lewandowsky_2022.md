# Yesilada and Lewandowsky (2022) - Systematic Review: YouTube Recommendations and Problematic Content

## Full Citation

Yesilada, Muhsin, and Stephan Lewandowsky. 2022. "Systematic Review: YouTube Recommendations and Problematic Content." *Internet Policy Review* 11(1). https://doi.org/10.14763/2022.1.1652

## One-Sentence Contribution

The paper provides the atlas's only systematic review of the recommendation-radicalization literature, concluding that most pre-2022 studies implicated YouTube recommendations in pathways to problematic content while also stressing that heterogeneous methods, weak personalization, and platform opacity severely limit what can be inferred about actual mechanisms (pp. 1, 9, 15-18). [✓]

## Research Question

Does the YouTube recommender system facilitate pathways to problematic content such as extremist, radicalizing, conspiratorial, or misinformative material, and what does the existing literature actually establish about that question? (pp. 1, 5) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** A systematic search of Google Scholar, Embase, Web of Science, and PubMed, supplemented by studies shared directly with the authors, plus narrative extraction of metadata, methods, classifications, and results from the included papers (pp. 5-8). [🤖]
- **Sample:** 1,187 candidate studies were screened down to a final sample of 23 included studies, which together analyzed 1,347,949 YouTube videos/channels (pp. 1, 5, 8-9). [🤖]
- **Geography:** Mixed and study-dependent; the review synthesizes papers across multiple content domains and contexts rather than a single shared geography (pp. 8-15). [🤖]
- **Period:** Database searches were run in November-December 2020, and the included studies ranged from 2013-2021 (pp. 7-9). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors conduct a PRISMA-style systematic review with explicit eligibility criteria focused on YouTube recommendation studies about problematic content, then use narrative synthesis rather than meta-analysis because the underlying studies vary too much in design, outcome measures, and classification schemes to be pooled quantitatively (pp. 5-8). [✓]

## Main Findings

1. Across the 23 included studies, 14 implicated the recommender system in facilitating problematic-content pathways, 7 found mixed results, and 2 did not implicate the recommender at all (pp. 1, 9, 15). [✓]
2. The review finds the most consistent support for filter-bubble-style reinforcement within problematic domains, whereas stronger radicalization or rabbit-hole claims from innocuous starting points remain more contested across the underlying studies (pp. 9-15). [✓]
3. Most studies do not model user personalization well: only four accounted for it, and the review treats plug-in or panel approaches as more informative than random-walk snapshots even though they also remain incomplete (pp. 9, 16-17). [✓]
4. The paper concludes that YouTube recommendations can facilitate pathways to problematic content, but that limited platform access and incomplete knowledge of the recommender mean current academic models may not capture the actual underlying mechanisms or consequences (pp. 1, 17-18). [✓]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The review explicitly assumes equal research quality across candidate papers rather than weighting studies by design strength, which matters because the included methods vary substantially (p. 6). [🤖]

The authors do not run a meta-analysis because the underlying studies use heterogeneous designs, outcome measures, and classification approaches, so the synthesis remains narrative rather than quantitatively aggregated (p. 8). [🤖]

The paper inherits inconsistent definitions of "problematic content" from the underlying literature, including cases where conspiratorial content is used as a proxy for extremist content or vice versa (pp. 5-6, 9-10). [🤖]

Because the search and inclusion window ends with studies available through 2021, the review cannot adjudicate later post-2022 platform changes and will age quickly in a fast-changing recommendation environment (pp. 7, 17-18). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This review should be read as a synthesis of the pre-2022 literature, much of which predates YouTube's more recent policy and product changes. It is valuable for understanding what the field thought it knew through 2021, but it should not be treated as direct evidence about the current recommender state after 2022 (pp. 7, 15-18). [✓]
