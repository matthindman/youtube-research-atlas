# Rieder et al. (2023) - Making a Living in the Creator Economy

## Full Citation

Rieder, Bernhard, Erik Borra, Òscar Coromina, and Ariadna Matamoros-Fernández. 2023. "Making a Living in the Creator Economy: A Large-Scale Study of Linking on YouTube." *Social Media + Society* 9(2): 1-20. https://doi.org/10.1177/20563051231180628

## One-Sentence Contribution

Using a near-complete sample of 153,000 YouTube channels with at least 100,000 subscribers and 137 million video descriptions, the paper shows that creator success is highly unequal and increasingly organized through cross-platform linking rather than YouTube-native monetization alone (p. 1). [✓]

## Research Question

How do YouTube creators use video-description links to diversify income streams, build online presence across platforms, and respond to the unequal structure of the creator economy? (pp. 1-2) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** A near-complete sample of 153,000 YouTube channels with at least 100,000 subscribers and the URLs found in 137 million video descriptions (p. 1). [🤖]
- **Sample:** "Elite" channels with at least 100,000 subscribers, retrieved at the end of 2019 (p. 1). [🤖]
- **Geography:** Global rather than country-bounded, with cross-national comparisons that include distinct patterns in countries such as Russia, Brazil, and India (pp. 8-10, 15). [🤖]
- **Period:** The channel collection was retrieved at the end of 2019, and the time-series linking figures trace activity from 2006 through 2019 (pp. 1, 7-9). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors classify linked domains into intra-platform, cross-platform, marketplace, crowdfunding, and other categories, then compare linking prevalence by subscriber tier, content category, and geography using descriptive quantitative analysis of channel- and video-level traces from public descriptions (pp. 6-10). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. The creator economy is highly unequal: the top 0.4% of channels account for 62% of views, and the lower tiers of the resulting income pyramid are effectively "working for free" (pp. 2, 4). [✓]
2. Cross-platform linking is more common than direct external monetization, which suggests that creators invest heavily in social-capital accumulation and brand building rather than only immediate income extraction (pp. 8-11). [🤖]
3. Linking practices are stratified: large channels use marketplace links more often, while smaller channels lean relatively more on community-oriented crowdfunding, yielding a tiered ecology of monetization options (pp. 15-16). [🤖]
4. YouTube's business model depends on a large reserve of aspirational creators, and platform pressures such as demonetization and algorithmic invisibility intensify an already precarious market (pp. 3-4, 16). [✓]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The analysis is limited to channels with at least 100,000 subscribers, excluding the millions of smaller channels that may face different monetization constraints and opportunities (pp. 1, 16). [🤖]

Link analysis captures visible URL-based strategies but misses sponsorships, product placements, and other income streams that do not appear in video descriptions (pp. 15-16). [🤖]

Because YouTube-native monetization is difficult to observe directly at scale, the paper can show creator adaptation to platform pressure more clearly than it can identify the full income mix of any given channel (pp. 15-16). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

The evidence is anchored in a pre-Shorts, end-2019 snapshot of elite channels. The authors discuss adpocalypse-era monetization pressure, but the findings do not directly speak to later monetization changes and platform-commerce features introduced after 2019 (pp. 1, 15-16). [🤖]

## Key Quotes

> "This ecosystem is highly unequal ... the top 0.4% of channels account for 62% of views" (p. 2). [✓]

> "The vast majority of creators are stuck on the lower rungs of what we call the 'aspirational curve' ... the lower tiers are basically working for free." (p. 4). [✓]
