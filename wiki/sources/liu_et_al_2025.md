# Liu et al. (2025) - Short-term Exposure to Filter-Bubble Recommendation Systems Has Limited Polarization Effects

## Full Citation

Liu, Naijia, Xinlan Emily Hu, Yasemin Savas, Matthew A. Baum, Adam J. Berinsky, Allison J. B. Chaney, Christopher Lucas, Rei Mariman, Justin de Benedictis-Kessner, Andrew M. Guess, Dean Knox, and Brandon M. Stewart. 2025. "Short-term Exposure to Filter-Bubble Recommendation Systems Has Limited Polarization Effects: Naturalistic Experiments on YouTube." *PNAS* 122(8): e2318127122. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2318127122

## One-Sentence Contribution

The paper provides the strongest causal test in the current atlas corpus that deliberately slanted YouTube-like recommendations change what users watch much more than what they believe, with little detectable short-term effect on policy attitudes, media trust, or affective polarization (pp. 1-2, 7-10). [✓]

## Research Question

Do filter-bubble and rabbit-hole recommendation environments on YouTube causally change users' media choices and downstream political attitudes, or are widely circulating algorithmic-radicalization claims overstated once user choice is held in view? (pp. 1-4) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** Real YouTube videos and recommendation networks on gun control and minimum wage, shown through a custom YouTube-like platform that records viewing choices, watch time, likes/dislikes, media-trust outcomes, and policy-attitude outcomes (pp. 1-2, 5). [🤖]
- **Sample:** Four experiments with a combined N of 8,883 participants, drawing on over 130,000 experimentally manipulated recommendations and more than 31,000 platform interactions; Studies 1-3 individually recruited roughly 2,500 respondents each and Study 4 recruited 1,032 respondents (pp. 1-2, 5, 11). [🤖]
- **Geography:** U.S.-based participants only, recruited through MTurk via CloudResearch and YouGov (pp. 5, 11). [🤖]
- **Period:** Study 1 started in June 2021, Studies 2 and 3 in April 2022, and Study 4 in May 2024 (p. 5). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors build a custom platform that reproduces a YouTube-style viewing session using real recommendation networks, then randomize participants into balanced versus slanted recommendation mixes in Studies 1-3 and into constant versus increasing-extremity video sequences in Study 4 to test filter-bubble and rabbit-hole mechanisms under naturalistic choice conditions (pp. 1-2, 5, 9-10). [✓]

## Main Findings

1. Slanting recommendations changes what participants choose to watch and sometimes how long they stay on the platform, but across the four studies those shifts do not produce substantial downstream changes in policy attitudes, media trust, or affective polarization (pp. 1-2, 7-8). [✓]
2. The strongest detectable persuasion effects come from the assigned seed video among moderates, not from the recommendation system itself, which suggests that forced exposure matters more here than the subsequent recommendation mix (pp. 8-9). [✓]
3. The rabbit-hole design in Study 4 finds no detectable attitudinal effect from increasing-extremity versus constant-extremity sequences, implying that any short-term rabbit-hole effect is smaller than simply being shown a conservative or liberal seed video (pp. 9-10). [✓]
4. The authors argue that these null or near-null short-term results shift the burden of proof onto stronger claims that YouTube recommendations polarize users at scale, while still leaving long-term and vulnerable-subpopulation effects open (pp. 1-2, 10). [✓]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The paper is explicitly about short-term exposure and says it cannot rule out longer-run effects from months or years of personalized recommendations or effects concentrated in small susceptible subpopulations (pp. 1-2, 10). [🤖]

The experiments are limited to gun control and minimum wage, so the authors do not claim that the same effect sizes must hold for every issue area or content genre on YouTube (p. 5). [🤖]

The design holds the supply of available videos largely fixed around existing YouTube recommendations, so it does not capture dynamic creator production or longer-run supply-demand feedback in the broader media ecosystem (p. 10). [🤖]

The study relies on U.S. survey samples from MTurk/CloudResearch and YouGov, and the authors note practical recruitment limits for time-intensive experiments of this kind (pp. 5, 11). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This source is best treated as mixed post-2019 evidence rather than as a purely post-2022 paper. Its fielding starts in June 2021, continues through April 2022, and only reaches 2024 in the final rabbit-hole experiment, so it materially strengthens the causal evidence base without cleanly settling the still-thin post-2022 bucket on its own (p. 5). [✓]
