# Reuters Institute (2025) - Digital News Report 2025

## Full Citation

Newman, Nic, Amy Ross Arguedas, Craig T. Robertson, Rasmus Kleis Nielsen, and Richard Fletcher. 2025. "Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2025." Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism. https://doi.org/10.60625/risj-8qqf-jt36

## One-Sentence Contribution

For the YouTube-relevant sections, the report shows that YouTube remains a major global news platform, that video news is shifting toward creator-led social networks, and that YouTube has become a key distribution channel for commentary-heavy news podcasts and talk formats (pp. 10, 17-21). [✓]

## Research Question

How are people across 48 markets accessing news on social and video platforms, which actors command attention there, and what does the rise of video, creators, and podcasts imply for the position of institutional journalism? (pp. 10-11, 17-22) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** Annual Reuters Institute survey data on news use, platform use, creator attention, misinformation concern, verification practices, and podcast/video consumption, with selected trend series back to 2020 or 2021 (pp. 10, 19-22). [🤖]
- **Sample:** Online samples across 48 markets, usually around 2,000 respondents per market, with network-specific random subsamples used for questions about where people pay attention on platforms such as YouTube and TikTok (pp. 10, 17-18, 22). [🤖]
- **Geography:** Forty-eight markets across six continents, with YouTube-specific discussion emphasizing India, Brazil, Thailand, Kenya, the United States, and the Philippines (pp. 10, 14, 16-19). [🤖]
- **Period:** 2025 survey fieldwork, supplemented by comparisons to equivalent measures from 2020 or 2021 where available (pp. 10, 19-20). [🤖]

## Methods

The report is a comparative descriptive survey synthesis that combines recurring Digital News Report questions with platform-specific attention items and country case illustrations to compare weekly news use, preferred modalities, creator attention, misinformation concern, and verification behavior across markets (pp. 10-22). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. Around a third of the global sample use YouTube for news each week, making it second only to Facebook among the named online networks in the report's overview (p. 10). [✓]
2. On video networks such as YouTube and TikTok, news attention is increasingly pulled toward creator-led content, and in the YouTube examples for India and Brazil creators rival or surpass traditional journalists for attention (pp. 17-19). [✓]
3. Video news consumption has risen sharply since 2020 or 2021, and in the United States YouTube's weekly news-video reach rose from 23% in 2021 to 32% in 2025; YouTube is also described as the main channel for podcast distribution in the United States (pp. 20-21). [✓]
4. The report ties this platform shift to broader concern about misinformation and influence: online influencers and personalities are seen as a major misinformation threat worldwide, even as official and traditional news sources remain important when users try to verify questionable material seen on social or video platforms (pp. 11, 22, 39). [✓]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The evidence is survey-based and self-reported, so it captures reported attention and concern rather than observed watch histories, recommendation pathways, or direct content exposure on YouTube (pp. 10, 17-22, 39). [🤖]

The report explicitly notes that the line between news creators and personalities is blurry for many respondents, which limits the precision of any hard category boundary (p. 17). [✓]

The most detailed YouTube attention breakdowns are illustrative country cases rather than a full channel-level comparative map across all 48 markets (pp. 18-19). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This is a current-platform snapshot taken in 2025, after the maturation of creator-led video news and after major platform pushes toward short video. It is useful for describing the contemporary news environment on YouTube, but not for making strong claims about longer-run historical trajectories before 2020 (pp. 10, 19-21). [🤖]
