# Reynolds and Hallinan (2024) - User-Generated Accountability

## Full Citation

Reynolds, C. J., and Blake Hallinan. 2024. "User-Generated Accountability: Public Participation in Algorithmic Governance on YouTube." *New Media & Society* 26(9): 5107-5129. https://doi.org/10.1177/14614448241251791

## One-Sentence Contribution

The paper argues that YouTube creators use publicity, audits, vlogs, and cross-platform signal boosting as "user-generated accountability" practices to make governance failures visible when official channels do not adequately explain or resolve them (pp. 1-3, 13-19). [🤖]

## Research Question

What forms do calls for accountability on YouTube take, what concerns do they address, and which actors do creators target when they try to hold the platform to account? (pp. 6-8) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** English-language YouTube videos about YouTube's algorithm or platform operations, collected through seed-video networks, 21 keyword searches, and manual additions from recommendations and references (pp. 7-8). [🤖]
- **Sample:** The authors collected 1329 candidate videos, cleaned that to 429 for coding, and then coded 250 videos from 172 unique channels until theoretical saturation (pp. 7-8). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The corpus is English-language and top-heavy toward large channels, but it spans many creator niches rather than a single content vertical (pp. 8-9, 18). [🤖]
- **Period:** The final sample includes videos from 2015 through 2023, with many of the key accountability disputes and examples concentrated in the post-2019 period (pp. 8-9, 17-18). [🤖]

## Methods

The study combines multiple sampling strategies, abductive codebook development, and dual-author coding to classify video format, stance toward YouTube, targets of accountability, and topics of concern, explicitly distinguishing between weaker accounts of governance and stronger attempts to assign responsibility (pp. 3-4, 7-9). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. Accountability on YouTube is usually creator-facing and discursive: vlogs make up 66% of the sample, the platform is the most common target of accountability, and creators often mix criticism with acknowledgment of YouTube's positive aspects rather than adopting a uniformly hostile stance (pp. 9-12, 17-18). [🤖]
2. The most common concerns are YouTube policies, platform metrics and optimization pressures, poor corporate communication, automated enforcement, and perceived discrimination against certain creators or content types (pp. 10-13, 17-19). [🤖]
3. Appeals are treated as necessary but insufficient, so creators often go off-platform to Twitter/X, hashtags, and other signal-boosting tactics to get YouTube's attention after official recourse fails (pp. 12-15, 17-18). [🤖]
4. Accountability power is unequally distributed: larger creators can use partner-manager access and audience reach more effectively, while smaller creators often need larger creators or outside media attention to make their grievances legible to the platform (pp. 14-18). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The study covers only English-language videos, even though large Spanish-, Portuguese-, Arabic-, and Hindi-language YouTube communities may face different accountability problems and publicity constraints (p. 18). [🤖]

Some notable creator communities are missing from the sample, including beauty influencers, so the paper does not provide a complete map of accountability talk across all major YouTube subcultures (p. 18). [🤖]

The visible sample is dominated by self-identified men, especially among larger creators, which likely affects the communicative styles and grievances captured in the corpus (pp. 8-9, 18). [🤖]

The article focuses on creator-led videos directed at YouTube and therefore does not cover the full accountability landscape involving regulators, NGOs, legislators, or other external institutions (p. 18). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This paper spans more than one governance regime: it includes accountability videos from 2015 through 2023, but many of the central complaints concern a post-2019 platform in which creators face mature automation systems, limited appeals, partner-manager asymmetries, and reliance on Twitter/X as an external pressure channel (pp. 8, 13-18). [🤖]

## Key Quotes

> "user-generated accountability" is "the generation of publicity via content creation to reveal failures, oversights, or harmful policies." (p. 1) [🤖]

> "YouTubers sought to address these problems by making noise, or generating publicity to draw YouTube's attention to particular problems." (p. 17) [🤖]
