# Abou El-Komboz, Kerkhof, and Loh (2023) - YPP and Content Supply

## Full Citation

Abou El-Komboz, Lena, Anna Kerkhof, and Johannes Loh.
2023. "Platform Partnership Programs and Content Supply:
Evidence from the YouTube 'Adpocalypse'." *CESifo Working
Paper* No. 10363. The archived corpus PDF is the working
paper version; the Batch 6 prompt cites a later
*Information Economics and Policy* publication. [🤖]

## One-Sentence Contribution

Using a regression discontinuity around YouTube's February
2018 partner-program threshold, the paper argues that losing
monetization access reduced upload frequency and lowered both
the quality and diversity of subsequent content among small
German creators near the cutoff (pp. 1, 17-23, 29-30). [🤖]

## Research Question

How did YouTube creators react when the 2018 Adpocalypse-era
rule change made YPP access more restrictive, and did losing
access causally change the amount, quality, and diversity of
their subsequent content supply? (pp. 1, 9-23) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** The study combines a December 2017 snapshot of active German YouTube channels with historical YPP participation, a second API snapshot collected from September to November 2020, and video-level metadata on uploads, views, likes, dislikes, duration, keywords, and categories (pp. 16-20). [🤖]
- **Sample:** The broader at-risk sample includes German creators who were in YPP in December 2017 and had 500 to 5,000 subscribers; the main estimation sample contains 484 creators between 900 and 1,200 subscribers who remained active after the rule change (pp. 16-23). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The empirical setting is German YouTube creators, not the global platform (pp. 16, 20-23, 29-30). [🤖]
- **Period:** The treatment is the February 2018 YPP eligibility change; the main outcomes compare the six months before and after the rule change, with creator histories observed again through late 2020 (pp. 10-12, 19-23, 29-30). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors exploit YouTube's February 2018 increase in YPP
requirements from 10,000 lifetime views to 1,000 subscribers
plus 4,000 hours of watchtime in the previous year
(pp. 10-12). Their main design is a regression discontinuity
around the 1,000-subscriber threshold, with watchtime
included as a control because true watchtime is not directly
observable from the API and must be approximated using a 50%
average-viewing assumption (pp. 16-22). The main local sample
uses creators between 900 and 1,200 subscribers, applies a
donut around the threshold, and compares pre/post outcomes
for upload frequency, like-share quality, and keyword-based
content diversity (pp. 20-24). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. Creators who lost access to YPP after the February 2018 rule change uploaded new videos less frequently than comparable creators who remained just above the threshold (pp. 1, 20-24, 29-30). [🤖]
2. The same treatment lowered content quality, measured through the share of likes among total reactions, indicating that monetization loss affected not just quantity but production value or audience reception (pp. 21-24, 29-30). [🤖]
3. Losing access also reduced content diversity, measured as the monthly number of unique keywords, suggesting narrower topic coverage after demonetization (pp. 21-24, 29-30). [🤖]
4. Heterogeneity matters: mainstream creators reacted more negatively than niche creators on activity and quality, while only more experienced creators clearly drove some of the negative post-shock effects, implying that nonpecuniary identity and status motives also matter (pp. 24-30). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The archived source is a 2023 working paper rather than the
later journal version cited in the prompt, so line-by-line
claims here are verified against the working-paper PDF
specifically. [🤖]

The design is local to small German creators near the 1,000-
subscriber threshold, so it does not identify the effect of
YPP changes on larger creators or on the full global creator
economy (pp. 20-23, 30). [🤖]

Watchtime, one of the two treatment variables, is not
directly observed and must be approximated, which is why the
main identification leans on subscriber counts close to the
threshold (pp. 16-22). [🤖]

The main sample excludes creators who exited the platform
before the second wave, so the estimated effects describe
behavioral changes among survivors rather than total effects
including exit (pp. 20-23). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This is foundational post-Partner-Program evidence anchored
to the February 2018 Adpocalypse rule change. It is directly
useful for the creator-economy theme because it identifies a
causal monetization shock in the early post-2018 governance
era, not just descriptive inequality in later creator markets.
[🤖]
