# Ørmen and Gregersen (2023) - Towards the Engagement Economy

## Full Citation

Ørmen, Jacob, and Andreas Gregersen. 2023. "Towards the Engagement Economy: Interconnected Processes of Commodification on YouTube." *Media, Culture & Society* 45(2): 225-245. https://doi.org/10.1177/01634437221111951

## One-Sentence Contribution

Through a document analysis of YouTube's public communication, the paper argues that the platform is moving beyond an advertising-centered attention economy toward an engagement economy that monetizes transactions across producers, users, and brands (pp. 225-226). [🤖]

## Research Question

How are commodification processes on YouTube changing as the platform expands from selling audience attention to facilitating a wider set of commercial transactions among users, creators, brands, and retailers? (pp. 225-226) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** Public communication from YouTube and related platform materials used to map marketplaces, monetization programs, and commercial interfaces (pp. 225-238). [🤖]
- **Sample:** Documents about YouTube's advertising systems, creator programs, memberships, shopping tools, and marketplace features rather than a behavioral sample of creators or viewers (pp. 229-238). [🤖]
- **Geography:** Platform-level and global rather than country-bounded, with examples drawn from YouTube's overall product and policy architecture (pp. 229-241). [🤖]
- **Period:** The analysis centers on features and documents current around 2021-2022, while also describing longer-run platform development (pp. 225-241). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors use critical document analysis to map YouTube as a "platform of marketplaces" and to track how the platform commodifies content, audiences, and creative labour through interconnected advertising, partnership, subscription, and shopping systems (pp. 225-238). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. YouTube is shifting from an attention economy centered on selling audiences to advertisers toward an engagement economy organized around broader commercial transactions among all major sides of the platform (pp. 225-226, 238). [🤖]
2. The platform operates as a set of linked marketplaces that include Google Ads auctions, BrandConnect, YouTube Select, memberships, digital goods such as Super Chat, and emerging shopping integrations (pp. 229-238). [🤖]
3. This expansion increases the value of creators and highly engaged audiences, but it also intensifies inequality by privileging commercially attractive actors over socially or politically important but less profitable ones (pp. 240-241). [🤖]
4. In the authors' account, YouTube is moving from intermediary to active editor, using YPP vetting and curated partnership programs to standardize exchange and cultivate brand-safe producers (pp. 234-240). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The paper analyzes public-facing platform documents rather than internal decision rules or observed transaction data, so it cannot show how these commercial systems actually perform in practice for different creator groups (pp. 225-226, 240-241). [🤖]

Because the evidence is drawn from public communication, it is especially strong on platform strategy and product design but weaker on the scale of adoption, creator uptake, or downstream audience effects (pp. 229-241). [🤖]

The article is a political-economic interpretation of platform change, not a direct measurement of specific creator sectors or national markets, so later empirical work is needed to test how far the engagement-economy frame travels (pp. 240-241). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

The document set is anchored in the platform features and product rhetoric visible around 2021-2022. It should therefore be read as a pre-2023 baseline for YouTube's commerce turn, not as a direct account of later product changes or creator uptake after those features matured (pp. 229-241). [🤖]

## Key Quotes

> "This process entails a crucial development in the business model from selling audiences to advertisers towards facilitating commercial transactions between all partners on the platform, a shift from an economy of attention towards an economy of engagement." (p. 226). [🤖]

> "The goal of matchmaking on the platform is shifting from maximising the number of connections to optimising the value of connecting. Popularity might still matter, but profitability reigns supreme." (p. 241). [🤖]
