# Hallinan, Reynolds, and Rothenstein (2024) - Copyright Callouts

## Full Citation

Hallinan, Blake, C. J. Reynolds, and Omer Rothenstein. 2024. "Copyright Callouts and the Promise of Creator-Driven Platform Governance." *Internet Policy Review* 13(2): 1-43. https://doi.org/10.14763/2024.2.1770

## One-Sentence Contribution

Analyzing 135 English-language YouTube copyright callout videos, the paper argues that creator complaints do two governance jobs: horizontal peer policing inside creator communities and vertical appeals toward platforms or rights holders that seek redress but offer limited institutional leverage (pp. 1, 16-19). [🤖]

## Research Question

How do YouTube copyright callout videos define copyright-enforcement problems, assign responsibility, propose solutions, and what do those public complaints reveal about creator-driven platform governance? (pp. 3-4, 7, 16-19) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** YouTube copyright callout videos collected through targeted keyword searches with YouTube Data Tools, plus videos added from recommendations and news coverage of copyright issues (pp. 7-8). [🤖]
- **Sample:** The authors built an initial dataset of 230 videos, then independently coded videos until they reached substantial repetition at 135 videos (pp. 8-9). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The corpus is English-language and multinational but centered on English-speaking creator spaces on a global platform (pp. 8-9, 18-19). [🤖]
- **Period:** The study restricts videos to material published after 2019, and the final dataset includes videos from 2019 through 2023 (pp. 8-10). [🤖]

## Methods

The study uses targeted keyword retrieval, manual screening to exclude off-topic or non-creator videos, additional video collection from recommendations and news coverage, and iterative dual-author coding of how videos define problems, assign responsibility, and propose solutions around copyright enforcement on YouTube (pp. 7-9). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. Creators primarily frame copyright governance problems around "false" claims, fair use, financial harms, harassment, creator wellbeing, and weak communication from YouTube rather than around a desire to leave the platform altogether (pp. 9-11). [🤖]
2. The videos emphasize voice-based responses such as appeals, going public, signal boosting, and workarounds, indicating that creators mostly seek redress inside the platform ecosystem rather than exit from it (pp. 10-14). [🤖]
3. Horizontal callouts target peer creators and function as community self-policing by drawing boundaries around acceptable conduct, especially around abusing copyright tools against other creators (pp. 11-13, 16-17). [🤖]
4. Vertical callouts target YouTube or outside rights holders and mobilize audiences toward fairness claims, but the paper presents them as reactive, uneven, and structurally weaker tools for influencing platform policy than formal participation rights would be (pp. 13-20). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The study analyzes English-language videos only, which the authors explicitly note is a major limitation on a multilingual and multinational platform (pp. 18-19). [🤖]

The speakers in the sample are overwhelmingly men, so the paper cannot say much about how copyright-governance disputes look across gendered creator communities (pp. 8-9, 18-19). [🤖]

The corpus is built from targeted keyword searches, manual additions, and coding to repetition rather than from a representative platform sample, so it is best read as a structured map of creator discourse rather than a prevalence estimate for all YouTube copyright disputes (pp. 7-9). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This is a post-2019, pre-DSA-access governance study: it covers copyright callout videos from 2019-2023, after major platform-governance changes were already salient, but before the EU's October 2025 researcher-access regime could alter outsider visibility into platform decision-making (pp. 7-10, 18-20). [🤖]
