# Verwiebe et al. (2025) - Unequal Earnings on YouTube

## Full Citation

Verwiebe, Roland, Chiara Osorio-Krauter, Nina-Sophie Fritsch, Sarah Weißmann, Aaron Philipp, and Claudia Buder. 2025. "Working on an Algorithm-Controlled Platform as a Content Creator: What Explains Unequal Earnings on YouTube?" *European Societies* 00(0): 1-29. https://doi.org/10.1162/EUSO.a.42

## One-Sentence Contribution

The paper provides a large-scale quantitative account of earnings inequality among monetized German-speaking YouTube creators and asks how platform-specific and sociostructural factors explain that inequality (pp. 1-3). [✓]

## Research Question

To what extent do sociostructural and platform-specific characteristics explain earnings inequality among content creators on YouTube, and which dimensions are the strongest predictors of earnings? (p. 3) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** A full sample of 22,466 monetized channels plus a random sample of 795 channels coded for sociostructural characteristics such as age, gender, education, and race (pp. 2, 9-10). [🤖]
- **Sample:** Monetized channels registered in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein, with smaller or implausible channels removed from the full sample and companies/media excluded from the coded random subsample (pp. 9-10). [🤖]
- **Geography:** German-speaking YouTube across Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein (pp. 1, 9). [🤖]
- **Period:** Channel data were retrieved in December 2022, monetization status was scraped as of March 2023, and the sociostructural coding survey ran from March to September 2023 (pp. 9-10). [🤖]

## Methods

The study combines exploratory data analysis of the full channel population with log-linear regression on the random subsample, using estimated average monthly earnings from YPP alongside platform variables such as subscribers, views, uploads, audience strength, and topic as well as coded sociostructural variables (pp. 10-18). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. Earnings are extremely unequal: monthly earnings range from $0.02 to $724,868, yielding a Gini coefficient of 0.89 in the full sample and 0.95 in the random sample (p. 12). [✓]
2. The top 1% of creators in the full sample (N = 225) capture about 46% of total earnings and average $37,011 per month, which strongly concentrates platform income at the top (p. 13). [✓]
3. Platform variables explain more of the observed variation in earnings than sociostructural variables: higher views, more subscribers, and more uploaded videos predict higher earnings, while older channel age and stronger recurring-commenter audiences are associated with lower earnings (pp. 15-20). [🤖]
4. The paper finds a gender pay gap disadvantaging women, no additional effect for education or race in this sample, and concludes that YouTube resembles a winner-take-all and superstar market rather than a broadly egalitarian niche economy (pp. 18-20). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The earning estimates depend on inferred CPM/RPM values and cannot fully account for unobserved factors such as audience geography or temporal CPM changes (p. 21). [🤖]

The study cannot directly observe how YouTube's recommendation system or demonetization decisions shape these earnings because the relevant algorithmic processes remain opaque (p. 21). [🤖]

Because the analysis focuses on monetized channels, it may understate inequality by excluding less commercially relevant or already demonetized creators who struggle to qualify for platform revenue in the first place (pp. 10, 21). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

The paper is a 2022-2023 snapshot of monetized German-speaking channels rather than a longitudinal view of the wider platform. It is therefore better suited to describing inequality levels and correlates than to showing how creator earnings changed after later platform shifts such as the March 31, 2025 Shorts view-count change (pp. 9-10). [🤖]
