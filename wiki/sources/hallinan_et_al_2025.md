# Hallinan et al. (2025) - Aspirational Platform Governance

## Full Citation

Hallinan, Blake, C. J. Reynolds, Yehonatan Kuperberg, and Omer Rothenstein. 2025. "Aspirational Platform Governance: How Creators Legitimise Content Moderation Through Accusations of Bias." *Internet Policy Review* 14(1): 1-28. https://doi.org/10.14763/2025.1.1829

## One-Sentence Contribution

Using a content analysis of 115 reaction videos about CoryxKenshin's August 2022 racism-and-favoritism callout, the paper argues that creator critiques of YouTube moderation work as an aspirational form of platform governance with visibility and solidarity effects but no guaranteed policy leverage (pp. 4, 19-22). [🤖]

## Research Question

How do creators publicly negotiate accusations of bias in YouTube content moderation, and what kind of governance participation do those reaction-video debates amount to on a centralized platform? (pp. 3-5) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** Public YouTube videos reacting to CoryxKenshin's "YouTube: Racism and Favoritism" video, plus metadata retrieved with YouTube Data Tools and associated online discourse (pp. 4, 9). [🤖]
- **Sample:** The authors identified 310 relevant videos through keyword searches, references, and recommendations, then coded 115 videos until theoretical saturation (pp. 9-10). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The conversation is primarily among English-speaking creators, and over 63% of sampled channels list the United States as their location (pp. 19, 14). [🤖]
- **Period:** The triggering video was posted on 24 August 2022, and the study analyzes the ensuing creator conversation as a post-2022 moderation controversy on YouTube (pp. 10, 22). [🤖]

## Methods

The authors map a reaction-video network around a single high-profile controversy, develop a codebook for creator identity, attitudes toward racism and bias claims, and evidentiary styles, then use dual-author coding on a theoretically saturated subset of 115 videos, usually watching the first 15 minutes of each video and coding channel genre from recent uploads (pp. 9-10). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. The discussion produced broad agreement that YouTube moderation is biased, but not agreement about what that bias means: more than 80% of participants endorsed bias claims while disagreeing over whether the relevant fault lines were race, ideology, geography, channel size, genre, or creator-platform relationships (pp. 16-19). [✓]
2. Creators trying to diagnose racism or favoritism face an epistemic problem because moderation evidence is sparse and opaque, so they lean heavily on personal testimony and comparisons to other creators rather than on verifiable platform-wide data (pp. 18-19). [✓]
3. Public reaction videos function as "aspirational platform governance": creators try to influence YouTube through publicity, moral appeal, and collective noise, but the paper finds no established channel that guarantees those efforts formal leverage over moderation decisions (pp. 19-21). [🤖]
4. Even when public callouts do not clearly change policy, they still create social support, information sharing, and pattern tracing among creators navigating opaque moderation and uneven procedural protections (pp. 20-22). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The evidence comes from one controversy centered on a highly visible elite creator, so the findings should not be read as a representative account of all moderation disputes on YouTube (pp. 10, 19, 22). [🤖]

The coded corpus is mostly English-speaking gaming, reaction, and commentary content, which limits how far the results travel across language communities, content verticals, or creator tiers (pp. 14-16, 19). [🤖]

Because the analysis is based on public discourse rather than internal platform records, it cannot directly verify why specific moderation decisions were made or whether any single public outcry caused later policy changes (pp. 20-21). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This is a post-2019, pre-DSA-access snapshot of YouTube governance talk: the case begins in August 2022, after the major API restriction period was already in place, but before the EU's October 2025 researcher-access regime could be observed in practice (pp. 3-5, 10, 20-21). [🤖]
