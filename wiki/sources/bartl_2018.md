# Bärtl (2018) - YouTube Channels, Uploads and Views

## Full Citation

Bärtl, Mathias. 2018. "YouTube channels, uploads and views: A
statistical analysis of the past 10 years." *Convergence: The
International Journal of Research into New Media Technologies* 24(1):
16-32. https://doi.org/10.1177/1354856517736979

## One-Sentence Contribution

Using a randomized YouTube API sampling strategy, this paper provides a
genuine pre-2020 platform-scale characterization of channels, uploads,
and views, showing both that large-scale descriptive work on YouTube
already existed and that attention was extremely concentrated among a
small minority of channels (pp. 16, 19-21, 26-30). [🤖]

## Research Question

How did YouTube channels, uploads, and views evolve across 2006-2016,
how concentrated were views among channels, and which channel
characteristics increased the odds of reaching a large audience? (pp.
18-20, 26-30) [🤖]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** The study uses YouTube Data API channel and video metadata
  retrieved through randomized string searches plus follow-up channel-
  and video-specific API requests (pp. 19-22). [🤖]
- **Sample:** Four collection batches yielded about 5,000 channels each;
  after deduplication, the combined sample contained 19,025 channels and
  5,591,400 videos, limited to channels with at least five uploads (pp.
  20-21). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The sampling logic is platform-wide rather than
  country-specific, though the author notes undercoverage of channels
  titled only in non-Latin scripts (pp. 21-22). [🤖]
- **Period:** The descriptive series covers channels created and videos
  uploaded from 2006 through 2016, using a pre-Shorts API environment
  current to 2016-2017 collection (pp. 20-21, 26-30). [🤖]

## Methods

The paper builds an approximate random sample by generating random
letter-string searches through the YouTube API, collecting eligible
channels, then retrieving associated channel and video metadata in four
large batches (pp. 20-22). [🤖]

Because API search returns can be popularity-sorted and capped, the
paper explicitly evaluates sampling bias by comparing searches with
fewer than 500 returns against broader results and by cross-checking
findings across batches rather than treating the raw sample as a perfect
census (pp. 20-22). [🤖]

## Main Findings

1. The paper is direct counter-evidence to any blanket claim that no
   earlier platform-scale descriptive studies existed: it explicitly
   attempts an overall characterization of YouTube using a random sample
   of channels and videos (p. 16). [🤖]
2. View concentration is extreme. Across all years together, the top 3%
   most-viewed channels account for 28% of uploads and 85% of all views,
   with year-specific dominance reaching nearly 90% of views in 2016
   (pp. 26-27). [🤖]
3. Older channels have systematically higher probabilities of reaching
   the top 3% most-viewed group, although some newer channels still
   break through quickly in favorable genres (pp. 29-30). [🤖]
4. The channel category mix and upload mix differ sharply: fewer than 3%
   of channels are News & Politics, but that category accounts for 45%
   of all uploads overall, while People & Blogs dominates channel counts
   in later years without dominating views to the same extent (pp.
   22-24). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

This is strong historical descriptive evidence, but not a full census.
The paper itself explains that YouTube API searches are not natively
random, that popularity sorting affects searches with many returns, and
that the resulting estimates should be interpreted with sensitivity to
sampling design (pp. 19-22, 26-27). [🤖]

The sampling frame excludes channels with fewer than five uploads and is
biased against channels titled only in non-Latin scripts, so it should
not be treated as neutral coverage of all global YouTube creators (pp.
20-22). [🤖]

Some of the most dramatic concentration estimates are presented as rough
indications rather than exact point estimates because API-return bias
can shift the top-3%-versus-bottom-97% split, especially in earlier
years (pp. 26-27). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This source is best read as a historical pre-Shorts baseline for what a
serious platform-scale YouTube description could achieve with the
mid-2010s API. It is highly relevant to the atlas, but it does not
describe the current platform and should not be used to imply that
today's YouTube remains equally measurable (pp. 19-22, 30). [🤖]
