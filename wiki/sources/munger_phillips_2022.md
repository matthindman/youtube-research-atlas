# Munger and Phillips (2022) - Right-Wing YouTube

## Full Citation

Munger, Kevin, and Joseph Phillips. 2022. "Right-Wing YouTube: A Supply and Demand Perspective." *The International Journal of Press/Politics* 27(1): 186-219. https://doi.org/10.1177/1940161220964767

## One-Sentence Contribution

The paper reframes right-wing YouTube through a supply-and-demand lens, arguing that creator incentives and committed audience communities matter at least as much as recommendation effects, then shows that far-right viewership peaked in 2017 before YouTube's January 2019 demotion of far-right content (pp. 3-4, 28, 32-33). [✓]

## Research Question

What technological affordances make YouTube distinct from other social media platforms and especially important to the online right, and how have the supply of and demand for right-wing YouTube content changed over time? (pp. 3-4) [✓]

## Data, Sample, Geography, and Period

- **Data:** Conceptual synthesis plus YouTube API data on channel uploads, video metadata, search visibility, view counts, comment counts, like counts, and dislike counts for right-wing and mainstream media channels (pp. 21-24, 28-31). [🤖]
- **Sample:** 35 channels classified as part of the YouTube Right and 219 mainstream-media comparison channels, yielding 26,058 right-wing videos with metadata and 821,840 comparison videos; search analyses cover monthly top-100 results on selected queries; viewership analyses track monthly trends and a May 2020 update (pp. 21-24, 30-31). [🤖]
- **Geography:** The paper centers U.S. political communication but studies a transnational right-wing creator ecology with actors in North America and Europe whose discourse often travels across national borders (pp. 4-5, 20-21). [🤖]
- **Period:** Search results run from January 2008 through October 2018, core monthly viewership trends run from January 2013 through October 2018, and the empirical update revisits those videos in May 2020 (pp. 22-24, 28, 30-31). [🤖]

## Methods

The study combines a conceptual affordance framework with descriptive analysis of channel clusters, API-derived video metadata, search visibility under selected political queries, monthly viewership trajectories, and comments-to-views ratios to characterize how right-wing political video supply and demand evolved on YouTube (pp. 3-4, 21-24, 28-33). [✓]

## Main Findings

1. YouTube is dramatically understudied in political science relative to its importance: the top three political science journals had published 15 Twitter-based quantitative articles but none based primarily on quantitative YouTube analysis (p. 5). [✓]
2. The paper argues that the main risk from right-wing YouTube is not a simple "radicalization by algorithm" story, but the platform's ability to help creators build committed communities around shared political canons (pp. 3-4). [✓]
3. Far-right viewership peaked in 2017 and was already declining before YouTube's January 2019 demotion of far-right content, while more mainstream-adjacent conservative creators expanded in production and viewership (pp. 3, 28-29, 32-33). [✓]
4. Alt-Lite and Alt-Right channels retained especially intense comments-to-views ratios even as viewership stagnated or declined, which the authors interpret as evidence of durable viewer community among the remaining audience (pp. 28-33). [🤖]

## Limitations / Scope Conditions

The study is intentionally about a right-wing political subsystem rather than YouTube as a whole, and the cluster assignments for the Alternative Influence Network involve some acknowledged subjectivity (pp. 20-21). [🤖]

The search analysis uses top-100 `viewCount` results and does not reproduce personalized search or recommendation experiences, which the authors note as an important caveat (pp. 22-24). [🤖]

The May 2020 update cannot fully recover when additional views accumulated and includes nonrandom missingness because some older videos or channels had been removed by then (pp. 21, 30-31). [🤖]

## Temporal Scope

This is primarily a pre-2019 paper for the recommendation-radicalization debate: its core right-wing ecosystem evidence covers January 2013-October 2018, with only an update through May 2020. It should not be flattened together with later post-2019 audits or post-2022 policy conditions (pp. 3-4, 28-34). [🤖]
