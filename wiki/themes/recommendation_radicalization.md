# Recommendation Algorithms, Exposure, and Radicalization

> Temporal scoping is mandatory here. The full 10-source corpus no longer supports treating "the YouTube algorithm" as one stable object across the pre-2019, post-2019, and post-2022 periods.

## Why This Theme Matters

Recommendation-radicalization remains the most publicly salient story about YouTube, but the fuller corpus now shows that the real question is not a single yes/no claim about rabbit holes. It is a set of linked debates about period effects, ideological sorting, non-algorithmic exposure pathways, and what outcome should count as the relevant harm in the first place. [🤖]

## How This Theme Is Organized

[[rabbit_hole_debate]] tracks the strongest public claim: whether YouTube recommendations pull users toward progressively more extreme content, or whether subscriptions, external links, and self-selection do more of the work. [🤖]

[[filter_bubble_evidence]] focuses on ideological sorting and its consequences: do recommendations narrow exposure, and if they do, does that actually move short-run attitudes or polarization? [🤖]

[[pre_2019_vs_post_2019_algorithm]] separates the regime question from the mechanism question: how much of the older rabbit-hole literature still describes the post-2019 or post-2022 system, and how much is now historically bounded? [🤖]

## Current Consensus

- Pre-2019 ecosystem evidence and post-2019 audit evidence should not be blended into a single present-tense claim about what YouTube "does." [🤖]
- The strongest post-2019 evidence no longer supports a mass rabbit-hole story for average users: Haroon finds only slight trail extremization, Brown finds few radical rabbit holes, Chen finds rare unsolicited rabbit-hole events, and Liu finds no detectable short-run rabbit-hole attitude effect. [🤖]
- Recommendations can still be ideologically congenial or mildly narrowing, especially for partisan or conservative users, even when they do not generate large extremization effects. [🤖]
- Exposure to problematic or extremist material is not zero in the post-2019 corpus, but the most consistent evidence points to reach concentrated in specific user groups rather than broad algorithmic radicalization en masse. [🤖]
- Non-algorithmic pathways matter a great deal: subscriptions, external links, creator-audience communities, and preexisting user demand are central parts of the exposure story. [🤖]
- The strongest causal papers in the corpus now point toward limited short-run attitudinal or polarization effects, even when recommendation environments are deliberately manipulated. [🤖]
- Recommendation design is not uniformly polarizing by construction: Yu shows that changing recommender inputs can broaden news exposure and reduce ideological congeniality without obvious short-run downstream harms. [🤖]
- The older literature summarized by Yesilada and Lewandowsky is best read as evidence about a heterogeneous pre-2022 field, not as a current snapshot of the platform. [🤖]

## Main Disagreements

- [[rabbit_hole_debate]] carries the dispute over whether the key mechanism is algorithmic escalation or a broader supply-demand and community process with only limited algorithmic amplification. [🤖]
- [[filter_bubble_evidence]] carries the dispute over whether ideological sorting should itself count as the central harm, or whether the more important question is demonstrable attitude change and polarization. [🤖]
- [[pre_2019_vs_post_2019_algorithm]] carries the dispute over how much older pathway studies still describe the current system after the 2019 ranking changes and later 2022-era shifts. [🤖]

## Temporal Scope Summary

- Pre-2019 coverage is strongest on right-wing ecosystem formation, community structure, and the 2017 far-right peak rather than on direct causal recommendation effects. [🤖]
- Post-2019 coverage is now the thickest part of the corpus: audits, browsing-history work, real-user traversals, browser tracking, and counterfactual bots all converge on a more limited algorithmic story than the classic rabbit-hole narrative suggests. [🤖]
- Post-2022 coverage is no longer empty because Yu and part of Liu reach that period, but it remains thin relative to how much current public discussion assumes about the present system. [🤖]

## What This Means for Our Project

- The methods companion should present recommendation-radicalization as a debate family, not a single stable proposition. [🤖]
- `paper1_attention_economy` and `study3_media_system` should keep supply, demand, subscriptions, and creator-community formation in view when discussing recommendation effects. [🤖]
- Future empirical work should combine exposure measurement with actual clicking, search, subscription, and longitudinal outcome data rather than relying on any one method in isolation. [🤖]
- The atlas now has enough source coverage to discuss this theme as a relatively complete literature cluster, but not enough to make strong present-tense claims about the post-2022 system. [🤖]

## Open Holes / Next Sources to Acquire

- More post-2022 and post-Shorts evidence is still needed, especially work that observes contemporary recommendation behavior for real users over time. [🤖]
- The corpus still lacks strong non-U.S. and cross-linguistic evidence about recommendation-radicalization dynamics. [🤖]
- Several foundational pre-2022 pathway papers remain un-ingested, including sources highlighted by the Yesilada and Lewandowsky review. [🤖]
