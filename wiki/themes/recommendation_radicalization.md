# Recommendation Algorithms, Exposure, and Radicalization

> Temporal scoping is mandatory here. The 16-source corpus no longer supports treating "the YouTube algorithm" as one stable object across pre-2019 growth, the 2019 transition, 2020 real-user evidence, and the thinner post-2022 period.

## Why This Theme Matters

Recommendation-radicalization remains the most publicly salient story about YouTube, but the fuller corpus now shows that the key question is not a single yes/no claim about rabbit holes. The live issue is how to connect historically specific pathway studies, transition-period audits, real-user behavior, comment-layer interaction, and short-run causal outcome work without flattening them into one headline. [🤖]

## How This Theme Is Organized

[[rabbit_hole_debate]] tracks the strongest public claim: whether YouTube recommendations pull users toward progressively more extreme content, or whether subscriptions, external links, entertainment drift, and self-selection explain more of what users actually encounter. [🤖]

[[filter_bubble_evidence]] focuses on ideological sorting and its consequences: do recommendations narrow exposure or public-affairs attention, and if they do, does that actually move short-run attitudes, deliberation, or political polarization? [🤖]

[[pre_2019_vs_post_2019_algorithm]] separates the regime question from the mechanism question: how much of the older pathway literature still describes the post-2019 or post-2022 system, and how much should now be treated as historically bounded transition evidence? [🤖]

## Current Consensus

- Pre-2019 growth evidence, 2019 transition audits, and later post-2019/post-2022 studies should not be collapsed into one present-tense statement about what YouTube "does." [🤖]
- The transition-period evidence is mixed rather than one-sided: Ribeiro et al. show historical migration from milder to more extreme right-wing channel communities and 2019 channel-level discoverability of Alt-right content, Faddoul shows default conspiracy recommendations fell after the 2019 demotion but did not disappear, and Ledwich finds a 2019 anonymous audit that mainstreamed rather than radicalized. [🤖]
- The strongest post-2019 real-user and causal evidence no longer supports a mass rabbit-hole story for average users: Brown finds few radical rabbit holes in fall 2020, Hosseinmardi finds moderation relative to matched user histories, Chen finds rare unsolicited rabbit-hole events, and Liu finds no detectable short-run rabbit-hole attitude effect. [🤖]
- Ideological sorting remains real, but it varies by layer: Haroon and Lai find congeniality in recommendations and media diets, Wu finds common but asymmetric cross-partisan comment interaction, and Huang shows recommendation harm can take the form of diversion from news toward entertainment rather than extremist escalation. [🤖]
- Non-algorithmic pathways matter a great deal. Subscriptions, external links, durable creator-audience communities, and preexisting user demand remain central parts of the exposure story even when recommendations contribute at the margin. [🤖]
- The strongest post-2022 evidence is still thin but no longer empty. Yu shows recommendation inputs can be nudged toward more diverse news exposure, and Liu partially reaches 2024 while still finding limited short-run downstream effects. [🤖]
- Audit results and realized-consumption results are not automatically contradictory: Ribeiro, Veselovsky, and West offer a coherent mechanism for why blind-following audits can overstate amplification relative to real-user consumption. [🤖]
- The Yesilada and Lewandowsky review is best read as a map of the pre-2022 field rather than as a direct statement about the current recommender. [🤖]

## Main Disagreements

- [[rabbit_hole_debate]] carries the core dispute over whether the best reading of the literature is residual algorithmic pathway risk or a broader story about historical community growth, subscriptions, external links, and selective demand with only limited algorithmic escalation. [🤖]
- [[filter_bubble_evidence]] carries the dispute over whether ideological sorting, entertainment diversion, and comment-layer hostility should count as central harms even when short-run attitude change is hard to detect. [🤖]
- Ledwich, Ribeiro, and Faddoul now make the transition period itself contested: the same 2019 moment can look like mainstreaming, residual conspiratorial recommendation, or channel-level far-right discoverability depending on the unit of analysis. [🤖]
- Ribeiro, Veselovsky, and West sharpen a methodological disagreement rather than an empirical one: how much weight should the atlas place on audit outputs that do not model user choice? [🤖]

## Temporal Scope Summary

- The pre-2018 part of the corpus is strongest on right-wing ecosystem formation, community intensity, and the 2017 far-right peak rather than on direct causal recommendation effects. [🤖]
- The 2018-2020 transition period is now much better populated: Ribeiro bridges pre-2019 commenter migration with a May-July 2019 audit, Faddoul directly spans late 2018 through early 2020, Ledwich audits a 2019 anonymous baseline, Brown and Wu add fall-2020 real-user and comment-layer evidence, and Huang turns 2019 Up Next dyads into a news-diversion account. [🤖]
- The 2021-2024 literature is the strongest source of present-tense caution: Hosseinmardi, Liu, and Yu are the best evidence that post-2019 recommendation effects are narrower and more contingent than the classic rabbit-hole narrative implies. [🤖]
- Post-2022 coverage is still thin relative to how often public discussion treats it as settled. Yu is clearly post-2022, Liu only partly reaches 2024, and the corpus still lacks strong Shorts-era or mobile-first evidence. [🤖]

## What This Means for Our Project

- The methods companion should present recommendation-radicalization as a family of periodized debates rather than as a single stable proposition. [🤖]
- `paper1_attention_economy` and `study3_media_system` should keep supply, demand, subscriptions, creator-community formation, and entertainment diversion in view when discussing recommendation effects. [🤖]
- Future empirical work should combine recommendation outputs with actual clicking, search, subscriptions, comments, and repeated outcome measures rather than relying on any single method in isolation. [🤖]
- The atlas now has enough source coverage to treat this as a relatively mature literature cluster for manuscript framing, but not enough to make strong present-tense claims about the post-2022 or Shorts-era system. [🤖]

## Open Holes / Next Sources to Acquire

- More post-2022 and post-Shorts evidence is still needed, especially work that observes contemporary recommendation behavior for real users on mobile over time. [🤖]
- The corpus still lacks strong non-U.S. and cross-linguistic evidence about recommendation-radicalization dynamics. [🤖]
- The remaining gap is now less about foundational pre-2022 papers than about connecting exposure opportunities to realized behavior and longer-run outcomes in contemporary settings. [🤖]
