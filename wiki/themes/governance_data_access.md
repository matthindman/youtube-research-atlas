# Platform Governance and Researcher Data Access

> Incremental ingest update. Still provisional until the Batch 5
> refresh incorporates the remaining governance source cards and
> post-2025 researcher-access material.

## Why This Theme Matters

This theme now combines evidence about creator-side opacity,
field-level access constraints, and a formal policy breakpoint.
Norton frames the research bottleneck, Hallinan et al. show how opaque
moderation produces creator accusations of bias, and the European
Commission announcement marks a new DSA pathway for qualified
researchers to request previously unavailable platform data as of
October 29, 2025. [🤖]

## Current Consensus

- External researchers and creators alike face major information
  deficits when evaluating platform governance, which makes confident
  diagnosis difficult even when grievances are public. [🤖]
- Research access has been a central bottleneck for YouTube
  scholarship, and the DSA now creates a formal post-2025 mechanism
  that could alter those conditions. [🤖]
- The new DSA access route is a policy fact, not yet an evaluated
  outcome: the current source set can identify the mechanism but not
  whether it works smoothly in practice. [🤖]
- Temporal scoping is mandatory because the theme now spans at least
  two clearly different access environments: the restrictive early-
  2020s period and the post-October-29-2025 DSA regime. [🤖]

## Main Disagreements

The current three-source set cannot yet tell us whether the new DSA
mechanism materially solves the access problem or merely formalizes a
new review process with uncertain payoff. Hallinan et al. emphasize
opaque moderation and aspirational participation under the earlier
regime, while the Commission announcement describes the new legal
route without implementation evidence. [🤖]

## Methodological Reasons for Disagreement

The three sources describe different points in the same governance
timeline. Norton and Shapiro diagnose the access problem from the
field level, Hallinan et al. show what opacity looks like in creator
practice, and the Commission announcement states that a new legal
mechanism now exists. The live methodological question is whether
future researchers can actually use that mechanism at scale. [🤖]

## Measures and Variables Used in the Literature

- Norton and Shapiro track platform coverage, geographic coverage,
  method mix, and researcher-reported bottlenecks in access, compute,
  staffing, and tooling. [🤖]
- Hallinan et al. code creator identity, channel size, genre,
  geography, claims about racism and bias, and the evidentiary styles
  used in public callout videos. [🤖]
- The Commission document emphasizes legal eligibility, systemic-risk
  relevance, Digital Services Coordinator review, and formal
  compliance obligations for covered platforms. [🤖]

## What This Means for Our Project

- The atlas can now mark October 29, 2025 as a meaningful breakpoint in
  the governance-data-access timeline. [🤖]
- Until implementation evidence arrives, the atlas should distinguish
  sharply between the existence of a formal DSA access mechanism and
  evidence that the mechanism materially improves YouTube research.
  [🤖]

## Open Holes / Next Sources to Acquire

- We now need empirical evidence about how often DSA data-access
  requests are approved, how uniformly they are assessed across Member
  States, and what kinds of YouTube data researchers actually receive.
  [🤖]
- The remaining Batch 5 governance papers should add creator-side,
  user-accountability, and media-pressure evidence so this theme is
  not reduced to access policy alone. [🤖]
